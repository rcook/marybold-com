Lecture on Publishing

— this is a good time to make faces and cringe and then get over it
Life isn't fair Publish or perish is a ludicrous requirement for academic employment. OK. Everyone agrees. Now, begin your publishing process.
Just don't talk You say you don't want to expose yourself to the world? Then don't talk about it. Just mail your submissions to editors who, believe me, won't remember your name past 1.5 minutes.
And I really mean don't talk Do you know how many writers talk their article instead of write it? Seriously, this is a common error. After talking about an idea for 4 months, the impetus to write about it dissipates. Then, the writer is prone to talk about "what I was going to write." If you have a topic, write about it.
Co-authors

Some people benefit from collaboration; others use the process to justify procrastination. Analyze your needs, strengths, and weaknesses and then choose the path of collaboration or solitary writing. Don't turn this decision into an excuse to not write "yet"; try writing solo and try writing with a partner. You'll notice which is better.
Set a schedule Go ahead and set an aggressive schedule. Submitting one article or query letter a month is fine to do. (You'll know in short order if you need to slow down.) To ease the burden, submit a mix of short pieces and long, a mix of research reports and book reviews, a mix of essays and lit reviews.
Go for quality Submit to quality publications. That doesn't mean that every journal is blind, peer-reviewed. Some fine publications are editor-run. You can afford to have both styles in your list of publications.

Similarly, you can afford to have both national and regional journals in the list. Are national journals "worth more" at tenure review time? Of course, but everyone understands the competition for getting into those journals. A mix of regional and smaller journals is expected to round out your C.V.

Must all your publications be scholarly literature? Depending on your field, you may find surprising tolerance for publishing in lay periodicals as well as lay books. One good way to justify publishing a "lay" piece occasionally is to say, "I needed the income to supplement my academic salary."
