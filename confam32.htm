Stepfamily—what Cherlin and Furstenberg predict will be the next traditional American family.  

The family field has researched stepfamilies for decades, now. Some study findings appear below, along with comments by leading scholars in the field.

(See also blended family.)

In a study of nonclinical families, stepfamilies exhibited lower cohesion and higher adaptability than nuclear families. The stepfamilies’ fundamental task was redefining the meaning of “family” (Pill, 1990).
Remarriage is not yet institutionalized but research indicates that norms are emerging (Ihinger-Tallman & Pasley, 1997). 
Basic stepfamily organization takes two years (Anderson & White, 1986). 
Stepfamily adjustment takes about seven years (Papernow, 1988).
Stepfamily adjustment takes three to five years (Ihinger-Tallman & Pasley, 1997).
The stepparent role is poorly formed and we seem reluctant to clarify it (Mason, 1998).

Step-family issues (Ganong, Coleman, & Fine, 1995):

1.      Complex, different histories

2.      Parent-child bonds older than spouses’

3.      Beliefs, expectations, myths

4.      Rules, roles, language, labels, names

5.      Hierarchy, boundaries, “binuclear”

6.      Co-parenting conflict

7.      Multiple subsystems

8.      Cohesion
