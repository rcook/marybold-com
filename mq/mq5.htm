Historical Perspective of Marriage and MQ

Timeline of marriage-related events

    Colonial times. Women born at this time grew up to produce 7 children (Gibson, 1993).

    19th century. Reasons for divorce were adultery, insanity, and union between blood relatives ("Class Notes," 1998).

    1850. The divorce rate was 5% ("Class Notes," 1998).

    1879-1948. Divorce was abolished in the state of South Carolina (Cook, 1998).    1880-1920. American cultural concerns were social reform, the endangered family, and industrialization.

    1887. First compilation of federal statistics on marriage and divorce began (Cook, 1998).

    1890. The median age at first marriage was 22.0 for women and 26.1 for men (Kornblum, 1994).

    1900. Women born at this time grew up to produce 2.3 children (Gibson, 1993).

    1903. The Inter-Church Conference on Marriage and Divorce convened and encouraged churches to prohibit remarriage (Cook, 1998).

    1906. The U. S. Congress convened the National Congress on Uniform Divorce Laws, which failed to convince states to relinquish their autonomy in marriage and divorce matters (Cook, 1998).

    1910.
    1. Blame for divorce shifted from individuals to industrialization, urbanization, and the women's movement (Cook, 1998).
    2. The median age at first marriage was 21.6 for women and 25.1 for men (Kornblum, 1994).

    1916. The U.S. led the world in the rate of divorce (Cook, 1998).

    1920s and 1930s. Women born at this time grew up to produce 3 children (Gibson, 1993).

    1920s.
    1. American cultural concerns were divorce, extramarital sex, changing women's roles, and the personal domain.
    2. 1920: The marriage/divorce ratio was 7.5:1 (Whitmire, 1997).
    3. Mid-1920s: Following divorce, mothers became the preferred guardians of children based on "the best interests of the child" (adopted from England's Guardianship of Infants Act of 1925) (Cook, 1998).

    1930. The median age at first marriage was 21.3 for women and 24.3 for men (Kornblum, 1994).

    1940s.
    1. 1940: The marriage/divorce ratio was 6:1 (Whitmire, 1997).
    2. Late 1940s: Women born at this time grew up to produce 2.1 children (Gibson, 1993).

    1950s.
    1. Functionalist theory held that marital quality was influenced by how spouses performed specific roles to support the function of the family (Hendrix, 1997).     
    2. Instrumental activities for men and expressive activities for women resulted in high differentiation of roles and (supposedly) subsequent high marital quality (Hendrix, 1997).
    3. American cultural concerns were security and a return to "normal" gender roles.
    4. 1950: The median age at first marriage was 20.3 for women and 22.8 for men (Crispell, 1994; Kornblum, 1994).

    1960s.
    1. Scholars called for creation of a theory of marital prediction (Larson & Holman, 1994).
    2. MQ study considered static, sociocultural, and family of origin factors (Larson & Holman, 1994).
    3. 1960: The marriage/divorce ratio was 3.8:1 (Whitmire, 1997).
    4. 1960: The proportion of births occurring out of wedlock was 5% (Furstenberg, 1996).
    5. 1960: 19% of women with children under age 6 were employed (Furstenberg, 1996).
    6. 1969: No-fault divorce law was passed in California (Cook, 1998).

    1970s and 1980s.
    1. The definition of marital success shifted from one of instability (typically marked by separation and divorce) to spouses' subjective view of their marriage (typically using words such as "satisfaction") (Clark-Nicholas & Gray-Little, 1991).
    2. No-fault divorce legislation spread across the U.S. (Wagner, 1998).

    1970s.
    1. This decade was essentially the end of research into homogamy (Larson & Holman, 1994).
    2. Couples' interests shifted from instrumental functioning of marriages to increased companionship in marriage (Erickson, 1993).
    3. The relationship between MQ and family stage was firmly established by many studies (Glenn, 1990).
    4. American cultural concerns were women in the workforce and alternative family forms (cohabitation, never-married mothers, stepfamilies, gay and lesbian relationships).
    5. The Uniform Marriage and Divorce Act passed (but has still not been adopted by all states) (Cook, 1998).
    6. 1970: The median age at first marriage was 21.8 for women and 23.6 for men (Kornblum, 1994).
    7. 1970: Of women aged 25-29, 10% had never married (Eschleman, 1997).
    8. 1970: Of men aged 25-29, 20% had never married (Eschleman, 1997).

    1980s and 1990s. Predictive studies about MQ shifted emphasis from spouses' individual characteristics to dyadic processes (Larson & Holman, 1994).    

    1980s.
    1. MQ theory moved toward an ecosystemic, dynamic, multidisciplinary way of thinking (Larson & Holman, 1994).
    2. Females were documented as performing approximately twice as much household labor as males (Blair, 1993).
    3. Research showed that MQ dropped with the couple's transition to parenthood, and that the change was greater for wives than for husbands. Continued research pointed to the change being a function of duration-of-marriage effects rather than the transition to parenthood (Glenn, 1990).
    4. The decade saw a steep increase in the number of studies about remarriage (Glenn, 1990).
    5. 1980: The marriage/divorce ratio was 2.3:1 (Whitmire, 1997).
    6. 1980: Of women aged 25-29, 20% had never married (Eschleman, 1997).
    7. 1980: of men aged 25-29, 33% had never married (Eschleman, 1997).

    1990s.
    1. 1990: 59% of married women with preschoolers were employed [1992 Census].
    2. 1990: 74% of married women with children aged 6 - 17 were employed [1992 Census].
    3. 1992: Dan Quayle commented on Murphy Brown's pregnancy (Skolnick, 1997a).
    4. 1993: The proportion of births occurring out of wedlock was 31% (Furstenberg, 1996).
    5. 1994: The median age at first marriage was 24.5 for women and 26.7 for men (Furstenberg, 1996).
    6. 1994: 55% of women with children under age 6 were employed (Furstenberg, 1996).
    7. 1996: The marriage/divorce ratio was 2:1 (Whitmire, 1997).
    8. 1997: Researchers predicted that 60% of new marriages will end in divorce or separation (Whitmire, 1997).
    9. 15 August 1997: Covenant marriage choice under Louisiana state Covenant Marriage Law became available (Caldwell, 1997; Cook, 1998).
