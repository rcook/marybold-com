Terms and dimensions of MQ

  1. In a textbook, Scanzoni and Scanzoni (1988) described marital quality as an expressive dimension with three elements: companionship, physical affection, and empathy.

  2. In studying farm families, Johnson and Booth (1990) measured marital quality with three questions concerning marital happiness, thoughts about divorce, and marital communication.

  3. In studying effects of the economy on couples, Conger, Elder, Lorenz, Conger, Simons, Whitbeck, Huck, and Melby (1990), measured marital quality with husbands’ and wives’ perceived satisfaction and happiness with their marriages.

  4. Lorenz, Conger, Simon, Whitbeck, and Elder (1991) assessed quality in terms of happiness/satisfaction and marital instability in terms of thoughts or actions of divorce.

  5. Johnson, Amoloza, and Booth (1992) defined marital quality in five dimensions: divorce proneness, marital problems, marital happiness, marital interaction, and marital disagreements.

  6. Vannoy and Philliber (1992) defined high marital quality by a close and compatible relationship, making a distinction from marital satisfaction, adjustment, or happiness.

  7. Two dimensions of marital quality were specified by Huggies, Galinsky, and Morris (1992) in research regarding employment: tension and companionship.

  8. Booth, Johnson, Branaman, and Sica (1995) considered five measures of quality in their examination of the influence of religion on marriage. They were marital happiness, marital interaction, marital disagreement, marital problems, and divorce proneness.

  9. Rogers (1996), in researching mother and stepfather families, addressed quality with two assessments: marital happiness and marital conflict.

  10. Hendrix (1997) used the term marital quality is refer to social companionship, emotional and sexual intimacy, expressions of affection, and agreement on issues.

  11. Johnson and Booth (1998) used the term marital happiness to address a marital partner’s global feeling about the marriage and satisfaction with specific aspects of the marriage. The global assessment would consider a partner’s feeling of overall happiness. A specific aspect might be an individual’s concern with understanding received from a spouse, the sexual relationship within a marriage, or the spouse as a companion.
