Before 1900

  • Women born at this time grew up to produce 7 children (Colonial times)

  • Reasons for divorce were adultery, insanity, and union between blood relatives

  • The divorce rate was 5% (1850)

  • Divorce was abolished in the state of South Carolina (from 1879 until 1948)

  • American cultural concerns were social reform, the endangered family, and industrialization

  • Compilation of federal statistics on marriage and divorce began (1887)

  • Median age at first marriage was 22.0 for women and 26.1 for men (1890)


1900 - 1919

  • Women born at this time grew up to produce 2.3 children (1900)

  • Inter-Church Conference on Marriage and Divorce convened and encouraged churches to prohibit remarriage (1903)

  • U. S. Congress convened the National Congress on Uniform Divorce Laws, which failed to convince states to relinquish their autonomy in marriage and divorce matters (1906)

  • Blame for divorce shifted from individuals to industrialization, urbanization, and the women’s movement (1910)

  • Median age at first marriage was 21.6 for women and 25.1 for men (1910)

  • The U.S. led the world in the rate of divorce (1916)


1920s & 1930s

  • Women born at this time grew up to produce 3 children (1920)

  • American cultural concerns were divorce, extramarital sex, changing women’s roles, and the personal domain (1920)

  • Marriage/divorce ratio was 7.5:1 (1920)

  • Following divorce, mothers became the preferred guardians of children based on “the best interests of the child” (adopted from England’s Guardianship of Infants Act) (Mid 1920s)

  • The median age at first marriage was 21.3 for women and 24.3 for men (1930)


1940s

  • Marriage/divorce ratio was 6:1 (1940)

  • Women born at this time grew up to produce 2.1 children (Late 1940s)


1950s

  • Functionalist theory held that marital quality was influenced by how spouses performed specific roles to support the function of the family

  • Instrumental activities for men and expressive activities for women resulted in high differentiation of roles and (supposedly) subsequent high marital quality

  • American cultural concerns were security and a return to “normal” gender roles.

  • Median age at first marriage was 20.3 for women and 22.8 for men (1950)

1960s

  • Scholars called for creation of a theory of marital prediction

  • Marriage/divorce ratio was 3.8:1 (1960)

  • Proportion of births occurring out of wedlock was 5% (1960)

  • 19% of women with children under age 6 were employed (1960)

  • No-fault divorce law was passed in California (1969)


1970s

  • No-fault divorce legislation spread across the U.S.

  • Couples’ interests shifted from instrumental functioning of marriages to increased companionship in marriage

  • Relationship between MQ and family stage was firmly established by many studies

  • American cultural concerns were women in the workforce and alternative family forms     (cohabitation, never-married mothers, stepfamilies, gay and lesbian relationships)

  • Median age at first marriage was 21.8 for women and 23.6 for men (1970)

  • Of women aged 25-29, 10% had never married (1970)

  • Of men aged 25-29, 20% had never married (1970)


1980s

  • Females were documented as performing approximately twice as much household labor as males

  • The decade saw a steep increase in the number of studies about remarriage

  • The marriage/divorce ratio was 2.3:1 (1980)

  • Of women aged 25-29, 20% had never married (1980)

  • Of men aged 25-29, 33% had never married (1980)


1990s

  • 59% of married women with preschoolers were employed (1990)

  • 74% of married women with children aged 6 - 17 were employed (1990)

  • Dan Quayle commented on Murphy Brown’s pregnancy (1992)

  • Proportion of births occurring out of wedlock was 31% (1993)

  • Median age at first marriage was 24.5 for women and 26.7 for men (1994)

  • 55% of women with children under age 6 were employed (1994)

  • Marriage/divorce ratio was 2:1 (1996)

  • Researchers predicted that 60% of new marriages will end in divorce or separation (1997)

  • Covenant marriage choice under Louisiana state Covenant Marriage Law became available (1997)
