Timeline

before 1850

Human behavior explained by traditional beliefs, folklore, religion, superstition

1850 — 1900

Positivism the dominant philosophy of science

The first psychology “textbook” is published

Anthropologists conducting comparative studies and taking a moralizing stance

1880 — 1900

Industrial revolution in the U.S.

Time of U. S. industrialization + urbanization

Concern for social reform at a high, along with concern for the endangered family and industrialization

Ellen Swallow Richards (1892) calling for recognition of Oekology as “the science of our normal lives.” 

Birth of family science, tied to sociology + home economics

1887: first compilation of federal statistics on marriage and divorce

1900 — 1920

First wave of behaviorism as a theory of human development

First wave of feminism, extending into the next decade as well

1920: right to vote extended to women

Social scientists striving for value-free methodology in research

World War I

1920 — 1940

1925: “best interest of the child” considered in custody cases (adopted after the British model)

Family defined as “a unity of interacting personalities” (Burgess, 1926)

NCFR founded (1938) for the interdisciplinary field of family studies

Family study recognized as a growing field

Freudian theory spreading

1930s + 1940s: greatest growth of symbolic interactionism as a theory

American concerns: divorce, extramarital sex, changing women’s roles, the personal domain

Social scientists using both quantitative and qualitative research methods, including polling and surveys

1940s

World War II

At start of decade, 41% of families structured like the Cleavers (dad at work, mom at home, children born to the couple following the parents’ only marriage ceremony)

1950s

Korean War

Americans seeking security and a return to “normal” gender roles

Birth rate up + marrying age down

Americans moving to the suburbs

Nuclear family as an ideal form

Academics begin calling themselves “social scientists”

Groundbreaking work in the area of nonrepresentation of ethnicity and its effect on children

Early in the decade, wide use of a textbook stressing symbolic interactionism promotes family roles and family reaction to stress

1960s

Major social upheaval including protests on college campuses

At start of decade, 43% of families structured like the Cleavers (dad at work, mom at home, children born to the couple following the parents’ only marriage ceremony)

Ethologists study animal instinct (and behaviors by young children) to inform theories of human behavior. Ethology is considered a precursor of sociobiology, which emerged in the following decade, although much of the initial ethological research dated 30 years earlier

Research on parenting styles influences subsequent education for parents

Greatest growth era for graduate education in terms of funding

Rapid spread of theories of psychosocial development, cognitive development, and behaviorism based on operant conditioning

1970s

Mid-decade, women entering the paid work force in large numbers

Second wave of feminism

Decade of greatest change in women’s roles and divorce rate

Mid-decade, sociobiology emerging as a formal science

Decade of the globalization of scholarship

1973: researcher comments that a major factor influencing a theory’s popularity is the number of graduate students trained in the theory

1980s

Postpositivist view becoming dominant

Multicultural, Black, and feminist studies emerging in the family field

Greatest development of human ecology theory

1990s

Stepfamily predicted as the next traditional American family

New family form emerging: intentional childbearing/adoption by gay and lesbian couples

Among family scientists, no consensus about what constitutes a family

Fathering as a research area

Group socialization theory emerges
