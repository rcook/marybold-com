Alloparent means other-parent, typically just saying that someone other than a biological or adoptive parent is rearing a child.

The term might connote shared parenting. For example, a child who spends 1/3 of time with a step-parent probably receives substantial care from that adult. (See also co-parent.) The step-parent may be called an alloparent.

Over the course of the 1990s, attention was drawn to grandparents as surrogate parents—another good example of the term alloparent. For grandparents in this position, legal status is often blurred. Most do not seek custody (becoming legal guardian) of the children, often expressing hope that their intervention is temporary. (See also grandparent-headed home.)

Alloparenting by government is typically represented by foster care. (See also foster care.)
