Parenting has become the word of choice in terms such as parenting support, parenting education, parenting skills. We are seeing more and more program names replace “Parent Education” with “Parenting Education.” The change may appear slight, but meaning can be altered with an -ing. 

“Parenting” shifts emphasis away from the who of nurturing children to the process of nurturing children. Part of this equation is that our traditional definition of parent may not be appropriate for a family. Many children are reared by grandparents or other extended family. Some are nurtured by foster families; fewer grow up in group homes. All the adults in these scenarios are nurturing, or parenting, children. They just happen to not be parents in the traditional sense.

The older terminology:

  • parent training

  • parent instruction

  • parent education

The shift in language also helps remove stigma in programs of parenting education, parenting instruction, and parenting training. Older terminology implied that the family field sought to educate, instruct, or train parents—which could be interpreted as family experts assuming that parents were ignorant of proper parenting standards. Newer terminology makes an offer of education about parenting, instruction in parenting methods, and training in specific skills.
