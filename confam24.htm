“Latent relationships” is how Matilda Riley (1983) described the growing number of on-going family and marriage relationships that may not be active at the moment but carry potential for activation. Growth is due to longevity—the longer people live, the more chance for relationship change—as well as to the increased incidence of divorce and remarriage.

Ex-spouses’ relationship, and a myriad of in-law relationships, may be active during years of childrearing but become latent when graduations and weddings of children are concluded. The latency period may be marked by occasional contact or by none at all. The relationship does not end, however; it has potential for renewed contact. Such contact may come with age-related decisions about retirement or with invitations for contact by younger generations.

Developing communication technology such as e-mail and Internet family webpages may also promote reactivation of latent relationships. Such technology may extend a further reach than past generations’ in-person family reunions. Thus, even ex-in-law connections are more likely than ever before to be made.
