Cost of Publishing

The production budget for an adult, nonfiction paperback book regularly exceeds $25,000. Budgets grow according to style of book (hardcover, illustrated, color) and editing requirements (reference material, textbook) and size of market (limited audiences driving the cost up the most). So, a budget of $50,000 or $75,000 is pretty routine. Those figures also explain why so few authors are published: every book represents an investment of money by a publisher.

By contrast, the cost of launching a web page is about $200. Even a well-designed commercial site with many bells and whistles can be maintained for about $5,000 a year. These are extremely low start-up costs and do not fully translate for scholarly or research-related publishing. (The researchers benefitting most from the low cost of electronic publishing are individual university faculty and organizations. Since most of what they publish themselves is not peer-reviewed material, editorial costs are relatively low.)

Scholarly publishing—of a journal, for example—on the Internet obviously saves on the physical costs of PP&B (paper, printing, and binding), but editorial costs are the same as in the print world. For what is to many publishers a painful transition from paper to electronic forms, editorial costs have actually risen. Once the investment in hardware, software, and training is complete, peer-review journals will start reaping the benefits of paperless publishing. Electronic journals are already well established in several disciplines, but in the Family Sciences such a transition has not occurred.

Skeptics fairly ask, isn’t the best material still in print? Will scholarly press ever really go on-line? At present, yes, print sources of scholarly work outnumber electronic sources. But economics alone will drive the transition to electronic journals and electronic libraries. Once on-line, journals and subscribers alike will add immediacy to their reasons for supporting the change in format.

Two Meanings of Filter

Traditional meaning: bad information is filtered out of the publishing pipeline through editorial review, peer review for journal articles, editorial supervision for books, even cost analyses by CPAs. Bad research, bad writing, mean-spirited material…all are kept to minimum through the normal filtering process. On the Internet, of course, little or no filtering is guaranteed.

High-tech meaning: as the volume of information increases, we need filtering devices to preview the information and select only that which we want/need to see. For example, a therapist who wants to keep abreast of research, readings, and statistics on domestic violence needs a robot program in the computer to filter the new information coming online and then tag and organize anything related to domestic violence. (Such a robot is sometimes called an information agent or news filter.)

Some search engines currently offer free news-sorting services. For example, My Yahoo is a customizable “home” page that you can program to automatically load your preferred news topics every time you log on to the Internet. (You can also have automatic updates on weather in several cities, your stock market holdings, your favorite sports teams, etc.)

Evolutionary Scheme of Things

Assuming that the Internet is a growing thing (known) which can improve (hoped), we can look for progress in the future. Some progress is tied to technology: as Internet backbones develop, we gain speed and power. Beyond that, progress is hard to predict, but presumably capability will include tricks we literally haven’t thought of yet. Automatic filing of journal articles we tag? Automatic filing of relevant articles we don’t tag? Automatic e-mail to distribute same? Information agents that can predict our interest in a new topic? All of these activities are possible now, with simple pre-programming. They may soon be possible with much less pre-programming.

Information scientists are currently working on faster, smarter ways to build hierarchies of useful websites without requiring human evaluation. Some tools that we are accustomed to seeing in citation indexing are being used, but new artificial intelligence tools are being developed, too.

A crucial element in this evolution of information handling is the librarian. As a curator of information, the librarian must be skilled at not only locating reserves of knowledge but also at creating access for other people. For example, a university librarian today cannot merely take requests from faculty members for literature searches. While a great arrangement for university faculty, the old system bogs down: the literature base is deeper and wider now and the librarian cannot be expected to make all the search decisions. Too, the librarian’s time is more limited today: just keeping up with new technology requires continuing education and training. The librarian must, then, find ways to facilitate the faculty’s access and use of information technology.

Librarian’s Nightmare

Our current status in the cataloging of information is a librarian’s nightmare. The companies behind the major search engines (DEC, for example) will certainly influence the future direction of organization. What we can say for certain is that organizing the flow of information is beyond the scope of the Dewey Decimal System and the Library of Congress System. In the first place, those systems were designed for different technologies. In the second place, they represent our American traditions. The Internet is an international entity. Our Western habits and preferences are merely that…habits and preferences.

One habit of just a few hundred years is our expectation of permanency of a library of printed information. Relatively few things are permanent on the Internet! Our keepers of information are faced with the task of archiving sound and animation in addition to more familiar written and visual works. For scholars, the temporariness of many Internet publications prevents use of material that must be, by academic definition, retrievable. Scholars may need to start archiving Internet materials themselves if they are not sure that their academic libraries are preserving materials in their field.

Evaluating What’s There

Whole courses (actually, whole degrees) speak to the question of how to evaluate information sources. Evaluating electronic sources, then, deserves more study than this overview. Still, most of us—with no formal training in the discipline—will be expected to be able to evaluate information, select sources, and recommend web sites to customers or clients.

Teaching Undergrads

As quickly as we learn how to evaluate electronic sources ourselves, we are asked to guide undergraduate students in doing the same. In some courses, our goal is simply to introduce students to the Internet and begin the necessary skill-building which will allow them to use computers in study and in employment. Eventually, our goal must extend to the evaluation of information. In the family sciences, this is a double challenge. The ambiguity of much of family sciences knowledge confounds evaluation.

Drs. Martin, Engelbrecht, and Chenoweth speak to this issue in their article “Effectively Teaching University Family Life Education: Selected Methods” (published in Family Science Review, Feb/May 1994). The methods reviewed are all classroom techniques which allow students to “experience the ambiguity of the content. [Students] readily see that answers to family life are beyond the textbook.” As we increase our use of the Internet in research and education, we will find new applications of the TWU professors’ observation on “the ambiguity of the content.”

What’s in it for the Family Sciences?

  • Anonymous Help Lines, 24 hours a day, for families and individuals
  • Chat rooms, hosted by qualified family scientists and therapists
  • Contact with audiences we’ve never seen in parent education programs
  • Education programs in a variety of styles to match the needs of a variety of audiences
  • Faster and cheaper access to research
  • Communication with peers
  • Access to theses and dissertations
  • Distance education, distance degrees

Common uses of information technology in Family Sciences departments:

Asking graduate students to explore research resources, with literally a global perspective.

Asking graduate students to create websites for access by the public. At Brigham Young University, graduate students earn credit in a one-semester course responsible for the website, FatherWork. (URL: http://fatherwork.byu.edu)

Evaluation of parenting education web sites.

Helping students find entire articles available on the Internet, thereby reducing trips to the Library.

Conducting a desktop video-conference with a colleague in New England or England, or perhaps bringing the desktop camera into hard-wired classrooms in order to continue the conference with graduate students’ participation.

Asking practicum students to describe their assignments in various ways, including using city maps easily available on the Internet. At a more advanced level, asking practicum students to demonstrate their assignments by posting live action on the Intenet.

Delivering curriculum to undergraduate students electronically, thereby promoting the use of classroom time for the processing (not just delivery) of information. 

For university child care centers, servicing parents with the ultimate school newsletter—one that can even provide live action of children in class.

Archiving WWW publications on research topics in professors’ own filing systems on their hard drives or burned on CD-ROMs for virtually permanent storage.

For students, accessing through department homepages all their department publications: course syllabi, department news bulletins, course rotation schedules, applications and other forms, student handbooks, etc.
