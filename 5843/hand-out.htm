Vocabulary & Concepts

Mothering

amae — desire to be loved or cared for unconditionally, desire to be dependent

Japanese mothers are "the best Jewish mothers in the world." — Garfinkel, P. (1983, September). The best Jewish mother in the world, Psychology Today, 17, 56-60.

taikyo — education in the womb; assumes positive impact is possible before birth

harmony — more highly valued than individualism

Classroom

White, M. (1987). The Japanese educational challenge. New York: The Free Press.

42 children per teacher

(Compared to American teachers ) Teacher delegates more, intervenes less, does not expect quiet in the classroom, gives fewer verbals cues, uses more large-group activities

Goal in classroom is harmony, not competition. 

Teachers use positive tone with children most of the time. Direct punishment is rare.

Lunch is served in the classroom, with children performing serving and cleaning tasks.

Chairs and tables may be rearranged several times in the course of the day.

In lessons, "mastery learning" is a common theme. A teacher's verbal expressions are seen to get in the way of learning (in contrast to the American teacher who typically begins a lesson with an explanation).

kyoshitsu okoku — classroom kingdom of equals (modern model)

vertical equality — traditional model of simultaneous one-to-one relationships between teacher and all students (like the traditional cormorant fishermen who keep their long-necked black birds on separate cords as they dive for fish; the fisherman can handle each bird separately but mind the entire flock at the same time)

han — work team; also used for "learning teams" because teachers respect the power of group learning. A slower child may be teamed with a group of high achievers. (Americans are more likely to use one-on-one peer tutoring

Homework

Tutoring

juku — supplementary help in academic subjects; utilized by 85% of elementary students and more than 90% of high school students

okeikogoto — enrichment lessons

trickle down — pressure has trickled down the parents being tested for entry of their child to the best nursery schools (America's equivalent is Manhattan's system of entry to exclusive feeder schools to the best prep schools, which are presumably feeders to the best universities)

ranjuku jidai — runaway juku

gakushu juku — private remedial classes

shingaku juku — examination cram classes

yobiko — cram school for student taking a year off before going to the next level of schooling
