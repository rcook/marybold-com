 About P.E.P.S.

 

The Program

As a program of The Parenting Center, PEPS extends the goal of prevention of child abuse and neglect to the community of adolescents. Although some of the adolescents addressed by PEPS are parents themselves, most are not. In fact, most of this audience will not take on parenting roles for five or more years. The challenge of delivering a preventive curriculum such as PEPS is that it does not occur at the recipient's "teachable moment," or the time of highest interest in the topic.

Yet adolescents are interested in many of the aspects of family life that can be associated with child abuse. PEPS seeks to tap those interests through curriculum and classroom delivery well-suited to the adolescents. While some abuse topics are addressed directly, others are presented indirectly. For example, sexual abuse is discussed in frank and open terms on an adult level. Physical abuse is addressed indirectly: discussion centers on how families handle stress, anxiety, and expectations of children's behavior. In some classrooms, students will move that discussion to the question of physical abuse. In others, physical abuse may be mentioned only briefly but the elements that frequently trigger abuse will have been covered thoroughly.

Much of this approach falls under the category of family strengths model (Dunst, 1995). While The Parenting Center's focus is firmly rooted in prevention, and it's practice includes intervention, the goal is sometimes best reached through a strengths model. In programs such as PEPS, a strengths model supports growth toward family life that never includes abuse.

The benefits of a family strengths model are tremendous (Bold, 2001).

  • People find their own philosophy about family.
  • People gain confidence in their ability to solve problems.
  • People learn how to ask for help when they need it.
  • People feel good about family and know how to strive for a happy one. 
This model supports "parents in their own growth as competent, caring people who can translate their philosophy and knowledge of family to future needs as they arise" (Bold, 2001). PEPS seeks to support adolescents in similar growth that will eventually influence how they maintain their own families in adulthood.

While PEPS does not elicit reports of abuse by students, the presentation of material does often provoke adolescents' decisions to seek help concerning abuse. Typically, this decision is made by a female student who decides to tell someone (the PEPS Educator, the classroom teacher, or the school counselor)  about sexual abuse she has experienced. In cases such as this, PEPS has effectively served as an intervention program. Because of this, PEPS Educators are trained in responding to reports of abuse.

Administration of PEPS

PEPS is administered by a Coordinator based in the Forth Worth office of The Parenting Center.

PEPS Educators

The PEPS curriculum is delivered by Educators trained and evaluated by the Coordinator.

The PEPS Curriculum

As detailed within this training site, the PEPS curriculum is a research-based program that addresses adolescents in a developmentally appropriate manner. Content knowledge reflects accepted standards in the field of family life education as well as the values and mission of The Parenting Center. Topics are relevant to most adolescents and answer the information needs of adolescents. 

Methods of curriculum delivery allow the PEPS Educator to serve as a facilitator of the group rather than as a lecturer. Although the PEPS Educator does sometimes impart information directly, indirect information delivery is the usual method. The theoretical foundation for the PEPS curriculum delivery is cognitive field interaction theory, with emphasis on exploratory-understanding learning. Strategies are employed to enhance knowledge transfer and to accommodate differing learning styles.

Certain aspects of the curriculum are based on results of focus groups conducted by The Parenting Center in 2001. Groups of PEPS educators (both paid employees and volunteers) and professionals from The Parenting Center were interviewed by an independent researcher. Comments from the focus groups guided the writing of this curriculum.

Classroom Health teachers familiar with the PEPS program were surveyed by the same researcher. Themes emerged from the survey that supported many of the needs identified by The Parenting Center.

The curriculum also reflects many of the elements of PEPS curricula from previous years. While data from the earliest programs (dating to 1980) are no longer current, basic precepts of PEPS have been retained or updated in order to maintain the historical reputation of the program in the community.

The Audience

PEPS is taught across two large school districts in the DFW Metroplex: Fort Worth Independent School District and Arlington Independent School District.

Ages of students range from 14 to 19. Grades range from 9 to 12; in most schools today, the majority of Health students are in grades 9 and 10.

Evaluation of the program

Evaluation of the program is accomplished through mixed methods:

Formative evaluation:

Quantitative evaluation of student's knowledge gain (end-of-week test as well as pre-test administered in some classrooms)

Qualitative evaluation of student's knowledge gain (essay section on end-of-week test)

Quantitative evaluation of program delivery across two school districts (voluntary evaluation that is completed by most students)

Qualitative evaluation of program delivery across two school districts (comment section on voluntary evaluation that is completed by most students)

Qualitative evaluation of program delivery by classroom teachers (end-of-week questionnaire completed after every PEPS presentation; completed by all teachers)

Qualitative evaluation by PEPS Educators (end-of-week questionnaire completed by most Educators)

Ongoing program evaluation by the PEPS Coordinator

Ongoing program evaluation by the Program Director of the The Parenting Center

Summative evaluation:

Quantitative and qualitative evaluation by the PEPS Coordinator and the Program Director

Outcome-based evaluation is conducted throughout these stages through the use of the following instruments:

Report of student enrollment and number of classes delivered

Pre-test (administered to selected classes) reported with numerical scores

Post-test (administered to all classes, except when school conditions do not allow) reported with numerical scores

Qualitative items on Post-test

Evaluation by Student (produces both quantitative and qualitative data)

Evaluation by classroom teacher
