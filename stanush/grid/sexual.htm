 Sexual Abuse

As historical forces go, sexuality education of any type is very new. University coursework in human sexuality was brand new in 1960 (Powell & Cassidy, 2001) and continues to be an elective for most college majors (including many education majors, which implies that the subject is not viewed as an essential part of the study of child development).

Sexual abuse is commonly categorized under "sexual health" as a sub-part of sexuality education (SIECUS, 1996). Only since the 1980s has sexual abuse been a focus of prevention programs.

The 1980s introduced many sexual abuse prevention programs, but outcomes continue to be debated (Powell & Cassidy, 2001). Evaluators have been concerned that abuse is highlighted with little balance by other sexuality topics. Thus, the messages presented in classrooms are almost entirely negative and may create fear. For some students, fear of abuse may influence their interactions with friends and families and essentially reduce healthy expressions of affection. Even when interaction is not influenced, the negative images and concepts created in the classroom may affect students' thinking about their own sexuality, either at the time of the education effort or in the future.

Little retrospective data is available to follow this line of inquiry. Since negative impact has not been documented it is a concern that rarely receives attention in curricula review. (An interesting question in any review is to ask, "What is missing?" Obviously, it is a difficult question to answer!)

While sexuality educators cannot weigh negative impact of short-term abuse programs, they do report that many instances of sexual abuse have been reported and investigated as a result of classroom programs. Because of this outcome, abuse has continued to be included in sexuality curricula. An honest appraisal of other reasons for inclusion might acknowledge that abuse (what's wrong) is easier for adults to talk about than sexual expression (what's right). 

When sexual abuse is presented as a preventive topic,  programs tend to focus on the most serious forms of abuse. These forms have the highest consensus among communities as being unacceptable sexual behaviors (statutory rape, adult fondling a child, penetration of a child by an adult, pornography created with child models, and so forth). Less popular topics are pornography depicting adults (standards vary, and what one sexuality educator labels abusive, another might label harmless) and consensual relations between minors. These topics present "gray areas" for both educators and audiences.

The tendency to avoid "gray areas" may increase the impact of the abuse topic that is presented in the classroom. The impact may be increased in two ways. First, the educator may inadvertently escalate the concern for abuse ("I may not be comfortable about other sexuality topics, but this I'm sure of ... I'll make this hard-hitting.") Second, the presentation of only the most abusive behaviors denies that most behaviors (and the people exhibiting them) exist along a continuum. Instead, the presentation implies that a sexual behavior is either all bad or all good, a dichotomy hard to defend in any discussion of human behavior.

Designing and delivering a curriculum that somehow solves these problems may not be possible. A wiser approach is to invite educators to explore the questions on a personal basis. Assuming the educator's motive is one of helpfulness, that educator may be the best judge of what information is needed most by a particular group of students in a classroom. The following questions for the educator may assist.

1. Am I prepared to hear a student's plea for help in confronting an abusive situation? 

2. Assuming that my "openness" to hearing a plea is discerned by students, what message am I currently sending? Do I need to change it?

3. Do I consider sexual abuse a behavior that condemns the victim to a troubled future? (There is no right or wrong answer; therapists who work with victims vary in their conclusions about this.) Is my attitude obvious in how I speak about abuse? Should it be?

4. Do I emphasize clear-cut anti-social sexual behavior because I prefer to keep the discussion off the "gray areas" that cause me discomfort?

5. Do I emphasize my rehearsed lecture points and discourage questions/discussion from students? 

6. Do I know what to say if a student blurts out a story about abuse in front of other students? Do I know what to say if it is said privately to me?

7. Do I have a public and private response ready for other sensitive topics?

8. Am I emotionally prepared to hear students' opinions or concerns about homosexuality, premarital sexual relations (encompassing a wide range of behaviors, not just intercourse), birth control technology, access to birth control technology, masturbation, communication about sexuality topics with parents, communication about sexuality topics with romantic partners as well as casual partners, date rape, STDs, and similar topics?

9. Am I prepared to interact with students on any of these issues while also balancing comments that may be made by other adults in the classroom?

10. Do I know how to control a discussion (public or private) that risks violating classroom standards, scope of approved curriculum, as well as students' and my own privacy?

Much of the PEPS curriculum is based on the traditional "worst abuse" topics. Because of school districts' constraints (as well as those dictated by communities), this emphasis in not likely to change. In other words, sexual abuse will probably never be taught by PEPS as a component of a more general sexuality curriculum. Our topic will likely continue to be placed in "worst abuse" categories.

Nevertheless, PEPS has an impact on sexuality issues. This is so simply because sexuality is a prevalent theme for most adolescents. Even when non-abuse sexuality topics are absent from the curriculum, they constitute a current running beneath the abuse topic. Alert educators realize that the sexual abuse topic is being received by students very much influenced by that underlying current. Consequently, the tenor of the abuse topic will be tied—positively or negatively—to the unspoken topics.

As a stand-alone abuse topic, sexual abuse in PEPS risks the negative outcomes listed at the start of this page. Educators can lessen those outcomes by assessing their own "agendas" on the sexuality education front. Then, positive outcomes are more likely:

making a report of abuse

understanding that the perpetrator is responsible

understanding that the victim is not responsible

learning that help is available

learning skills that promote resiliency or recovery
