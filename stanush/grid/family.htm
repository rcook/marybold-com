 Family

Across the 1990s, family scholars became increasingly reluctant to define "family." Definitions based on family structure were no longer accurate as structure itself became more complex, especially with blended families due to remarriage.

Legal definitions also became more fluid. Blood relations were no longer cited as requirement although blood ties have continued to dictate in certain judicial areas, such as custody of children.

Although most printed forms continue to stress "parent" and "guardian" as terms to define a child's family, some schools (and orthodontist offices!) have included space on forms to list everyone the child spends time with.

The following information is not typically shared with students in the classroom:

The term "family" is almost never restricted to "adult(s) + child" in scholarly literature. Today's family scholar is likely to consider all these constellations an example of family: two adults cohabiting, adults living with children, adults living without children, an adult living with a pet, adults living apart but who consider themselves a collective family, etc.

A change in institutional views of family has been evident in large corporations, as well. "Marital relations" has been broadened to include commitment by two individuals, with employment benefits often the subject for corporations' definitions of beneficiary and dependent. Cohabitation and long-term commitment have thus sometimes substituted for marital status on "official" forms.

The short answer to "What is family?" today has become "Whatever you mean it to be." The strongest complaint against such a loose definition has come from conservative theorists who maintain that the structure or composition of family predicts family functioning.
