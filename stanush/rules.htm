 Rules & Regs

 
The Parenting Center's policies guide PEPS Educators in terms of responsibility to represent TPC standards and mission.

The curriculum guides PEPS Educators in terms of approved material for presentation in the public school classroom.

Typical questions and comments from students are presented with suggested responses. These responses serve as examples of "tone and tenor" that PEPS Educators should adopt in answering other concerns.

The curriculum also presents information that is typically not shared with students. While PEPS Educators are not "censored," this guidance helps delineate subject areas that are historically avoided not just by PEPS, but by all guest speakers in the public school systems. Because public schools are sensitive to parental and community disapproval of discussion of "controversial" topics, the ISDs have been reluctant to endorse certain curricula, especially those addressing sexuality.

Discussion of condoms, consensual sexual relations, and homosexuality causes the most reaction among parents and community. PEPS Educators do not initiate such discussions. However, when students ask questions about these topics, the PEPS Educator may choose to address them. This is a judgment call that must take into account the group dynamic of the individual classroom.

The PEPS Educator is always able to say, "That's not in my curriculum and I cannot speak to it knowledgably. I will discuss with your teacher where you can find that information."

Even when the PEPS Educator decides the topic needs to be addressed, a tone of open-mindedness and calm should be used. A good self-discipline technique: imagine that the school principal, a grandmother, and a minister are sitting at the back of the room. Make remarks that those people would consider respectful.

Cutting short a discussion can be handled tactfully with a comment such as, "Thank you for exploring that so maturely. We cannot solve the larger issue and we have to move on with the material before us. But I respect your ability to discuss the issue."

Classroom teachers have reported that follow-up discussions have explored issues that arose in PEPS. For sensitive issues, this is a good outcome: the classroom teacher knows the climate and culture of the individual school and community and can likely address the issue more bluntly than the PEPS Educator. 

The PEPS Coordinator assists PEPS Educators in preparing to respond to a variety of potential student comments. Preparation is especially needed in order to feel comfortable and confident in hearing students' reports of abuse.

Sexual abuse is sometimes reported by students after the issue is explored in the PEPS classroom. TPC protocol for responding to a report is to offer to "find help" for the student. This should include immediate notification to the PEPS Coordinator. It may also include immediate report to the school counselor.

PEPS Educators do not promote reporting abuse to the Educators, but the curriculum may provoke it. This is an important distinction, since PEPS is an education program. At no time do PEPS Educators present themselves as counselors.
