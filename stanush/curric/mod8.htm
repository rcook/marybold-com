Introduction to the Amygdala

Transparency 8a

Throughout our lives, our brains are important in how we process emotions. The first processing takes place in the amygdala.

Located in the limbic system of the brain, the amygdala is the center for our emotional reactions. We can experience emotions in other parts of our brain, as well, and some scientists posit that emotions are part of our molecular structure, throughout the body (Pert, 1997). But we're pretty sure that the reactions that produce the words we regret later emerge from the amygdala.

I'm referring to words like, "Stupid!" and "I hate you!" These are words that are hard to take back after an argument, but can be the cause for escalating an argument unnecessarily.

3 to 6 Seconds

Transparency 8b

The "regrettable" words are borne in a crucial 3 to 6 seconds when the amygdala is in charge. The rest of the brain is literally under the control of the amygdala (Freedman, 2001). Now, in a crisis situation, this is probably a good thing. It's the amygdala that says urgently, "Run!" or "Duck!" But in an argument or a moment of anger, the amygdala is not doing us a favor. It's leading us into language that will alienate our friends and family.

There is a strategy for damage control: distract the amygdala so that the crucial 3 to 6 seconds pass without any angry words being uttered.

You've heard of the old advice, "Count to 10" before reacting with anger. Well, that is a good strategy if counting to 10 is difficult for you. It works for kids because they really do have to think about the sequence of numbers in order to get it right.

But for adults, counting to 10 is much too easy. We need something difficult to distract us. So, we can put the brain to work reciting the capitals of states surrounding Texas, or the first five presidents of the U.S., or something else that strains our powers of concentration. 

More brain power is required for these tasks, and the amygdala is distracted from its instinct to strike out.

Once the 6 dangerous seconds have passed, we regain control of the brain and we can choose what we say, choose how to express our anger.

[Insightful students may comment that they like to express anger, that perhaps they don't want to distract the amygdala. This invites an explanation of catharsis and acceptable expressions of anger. While anger management strategies frequently include use of alternative behaviors as catharsis, such behaviors are probably needed only by people who genuinely struggle with explosive tempers. Most people do not require special strategies to "get out the anger." In a move away from past therapies that warned of pent-up anger, current thinking is that most people do not suffer when they choose to handle (rather than display) anger.]
