Introduction to Family Dynamic

How are people getting along in a family? We call that the family dynamic and it can sometimes be summed up in two words: communication and anxiety. If the family handles these, then the dynamic is probably pretty healthy. (Never perfect!) But we can't write a recipe for family dynamic. People are different! What works in my family might not be right for your family. We can see that clearly just comparing families' communication styles.

Family Communication

Transparency 3a

What's normal family communication? How much talking should a family be doing every day? I have no idea! Some of the first research that used videotape of families in their own homes was conducted by Ray Birdwhistell back in the 1960s and 1970s. One of his studies looked at families in different settings in Pennsylvania. He found a huge range in communication time among healthy families: from just 2½ minutes a day all the way up to 12 hours a day. 

That's a huge range! Sometimes family communication changes drastically when kids grow to a new stage. So, what was usual when the kids were little may be different when the kids are big. And talking time changes with the seasons, and with school schedules.

[Option: Can you tell me what's a normal amount of talking in your family? I imagine we'll find a range of talking time just in this room. Discussion may produce comments about sibling relations, decreased contact with parents, and busy schedules.]

A goal for family communication probably shouldn't focus so much on "how many minutes a day" as on "how open is it?" I want my kids to be able to talk to me when they need to or want to. And I want to be able to talk when I need or want to. So, when I say "open communication" in my house, I really mean "available for communication." 

I can tell you where "available" worked best for us. The kitchen, and I don't necessarily mean family meals because there were plenty of years that we weren't all home at supper time.

[Option: What places work for you and your family? Discussion may produce comments about conversation in the car, on family vacation, around the pool, and so forth.]

Regardless of the time and the place, family communication should be available to everyone in the family. When you need to talk, you need to believe there's someone who's going to listen.

What about fighting? Again, there's a huge range: the loud family who thinks Thanksgiving just isn't Thanksgiving without a good fight … and the quiet family where no one raises a voice, ever. Even the very quiet family has its fights. They just do it in another style.

What's fair in family fights? Should children never see their parents argue? Families are so different, that we don't want to write a recipe of "Just This Much Anger Is Allowed." One rule of thumb is to look at how the kids are handling it. If the kids are worried, then the parents are probably fussing too much. If the kids are taking things in stride, and if they see their parents settling an argument as well as starting one, then there's probably an OK level of fighting going on.

Conflict is a normal part of life. And anytime two or more people live together, there's going to be conflict! Conflict is inevitable for families. We all react differently to conflict. But most of us feel some anxiety when conflict arises.

Anxiety

Transparency 3b

This is a potato. And it represents anxiety. Most of us have a hard time defining anxiety, but we know it when we feel it. 

Anxiety is different from stress. Stress is something from the outside pressuring me. I can identify a stressor pretty well, even if I can't always eliminate it. Finals week brings pressure and stress. You can't eliminate that stress, but you can decide how to handle it. 

And anxiety is different from fear. When I have a fear, I know what's scaring me. I can point to it, or describe it. Sometimes, I can even decide to handle it.

Anxiety is different. I cannot point to it. I cannot name it or describe it. But I know it when I feel it. And a lot of people have a hard time handling it.

So, that's where the potato comes in. I imagine that it's a "hot" potato and it does a fair job of symbolizing anxiety. It's hot so I feel jumpy when I hold it. In fact, it's so hot that I act — I throw it off every chance I get. And that gets us to a fact about anxiety: it travels through a group.

Ever been in a room of babies and toddlers? If one starts crying, a chain reaction begins. "Hey, that baby must know that something's wrong. I'm going to cry, too!"

At a workplace, anxiety can travel in a day's time, sometimes just on a rumor that a change may be coming.

And in a high school, anxiety can sweep through in a matter of hours. And it doesn't have to be finals week!

Let's see how anxiety can travel around this room. I'll start tossing anxiety to this one person and let's watch as the "hot" potatoes move down every row of this room.

    

[Start handing the props slowly with the first one or two potatoes, then hand off potatoes more quickly to the starter person. By the time the last potato is handed off, start making anxious announcements: Faster, faster. That potato's too hot to hold. Get them moving. Hurry. More are coming. And they'll start coming around again in just a minute. Faster, faster. Allow the potatoes to circulate for a couple of minutes. At some point, people will start throwing them. Allow that for a bit, too.]

OK. Let's try to contain the anxiety, now. Just gather them together back there for me. 

Lowering anxiety is much harder because our instinct is to "pass it off." Feel bad? Make the next person feel bad, too. You've heard the expression "kick the dog"? That's anxiety talking.

The problem is, you can't really pass it off and feel better. Because it's going to come around again!

And if you're in a group passing anxiety, maybe a family upset about a problem, the anxiety is guaranteed to return. For the group, that's bad, too. Because as long as everyone is anxious, they're not going to be very good problem-solvers.

So. How to lower anxiety. If I can lower it, then I can attack the real problem. If my anxiety rules, then I'm never going to solve my problems.

Lowering anxiety takes awareness. You have to figure out whether you're dealing with stress, or fear, or anxiety. But as I said earlier, most of us recognize anxiety, even if we can't describe it.

Lowering anxiety takes patience and practice. It takes time and it may not work very well the first few times you try it.

There's not a single perfect way to reduce anxiety. Some people just calm themselves with self-talk. Some people meditate. Some people go clean the bathtub. I imagine myself holding a hot potato that I want desperately to hand off to someone else, but instead I start lowering it in my hands. It's still hot, but I hold it gently and slowly lower it as it cools off. 

Transparency 3c

Therapists tell us that, with practice, people can lower their own anxiety in a matter of minutes (Jennings, 1999). Most people can do that in 4 to 10 minutes. Now, not everyone will fit in that window. I'm married to a man who can lower his anxiety in about 45 seconds. It's great to live with someone like that! But a friend of mine takes about 45 minutes. She has to talk to herself to sort things out. Her anxiety drops very slowly.

[Option: Does anyone here know how you handle your anxiety or how long it takes? Discussion may include comments such as: I cry, I have to talk to someone, I clean the house, I go exercise for an hour, I just feel bad for hours. Females may be more forthcoming with examples than males.]

Recognizing anxiety, and knowing how long it takes you to lower it, is important self-knowledge. Not only can you help the process by reminding yourself that you know the anxiety will lessen, but you can also help lower the group anxiety.

Now, this doesn't mean you have to be responsible for everyone else's anxiety, but if you are in a group where anxiety is being passed around, it may be in your best interest to help lower the group's level of anxiety.

If you can first lower your own anxiety (in the 4 to 10 minutes), and then if you can maintain that for another 8 to 16 minutes, the group will respond. That seems to be about the amount of time it takes for a group to notice that things are different. So, the whole process could easily take 15 to 25 minutes, but then the group will be better able to solve the real problem, instead of just throwing around the anxiety about the problem.

There's no guarantee your group will do this in 8 to 16 minutes. A really dysfunctional group could take hours. I don't know if I could stay in that setting that long, waiting!

This may be something you can try. I hope it works for you.
