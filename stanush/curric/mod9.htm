Introduction to Resiliency

Transparency 9a

Humans are amazing at taking care of themselves. They are resilient, or at least they can be. Resiliency is "bouncing back," specifically, bouncing back after hard times.

In terms of abuse, we don't want to minimize the damage that abuse causes, but we also don't want to overlook the bounce-back that some people are capable of.

We think that about 10% of kids are "hardy." These are the kids who live in extreme conditions but make it, anyway. They seem to beat the odds.

And by adolescence, we see that as many as 1/3 of abused or neglected kids are turning their lives around. They learn how to build support networks, even if their home lives are not supporting. They reach out to friends or neighbors, maybe a teacher or a coach, maybe a relative other than parents. By reaching out, they create an adaptive environment for themselves. They don't just survive, just barely cope. They cope very well even in difficult situations.

Now, we don't just sit back and see which kids are resilient and which aren't. We want to support kids and families who are trying to move toward resiliency. We can offer support as resiliency skills are learned.

And the experts are certain that resiliency can be learned. We don't have to be born with certain traits (Walsh, 1998). We can learn to "live well [even] when … brought up badly" (Wolin & Wolin, 1993).

One way to engender resiliency is to become aware of how we process stress and anxiety and then create strategies to react to stress and anxiety.

I can tell you exactly what I do. When I feel anxiety (on the inside), I admit it to myself with gentle self-talk. I say, "Hmmm. This must be anxiety because I'm not able to even point to my issue. But I can settle down and let my real worry surface." It works after a few minutes.

When I feel stress or pressure (from the outside), I can pinpoint it immediately. "This is stress from the flat tire I just spotted on my car."

Now, even if I can't relieve that specific stress immediately, I can do other things that make my flat-tire day bearable.

For me, that means taking time during the week to get out to a movie. I go alone. I love to go to a matinee alone, so I don't have to talk to anyone. I can even go to bad movies because I don't have to listen to a companion putting them down. I love bad movies. 

Transparency 9b

I'm going to ask you to brainstorm as many strategies as you can think of to innoculate yourselves against stress and anxiety. Work in groups of 4 or 5 to fill in as many squares as you can on sheets like this one. 

You'll find spaces to write in things that cost you money, either expensive (put those in the squares with dollar signs) or cheap (put the cheap things in the squares with cent signs).

And you'll have another sheet for entering things you can do for free.

[Distribute bingo cards + markers. Circulate and help any groups that are stalled. Listen in on every group and find something to praise.]

It's sharing time. What group wants to report first? [If time allows, ask groups to report from an overhead transparency so that everyone can see their entries. Help count their entries and announce total numbers, leading the applause for every announcement. When subsequent groups report, duplicate entries will be obvious, so comment should be made on the differing entries. If a late-reporting group has added entries upon seeing early-reporting groups' work, laugh and encourage applause for the fast-thinkers who have learned so many "extra" things in just the past few minutes. If entries are specific to ethnicity or religion, take the opportunity to "broaden" the concepts. For example, "Yes, church is a great example; many people find rejuvenation by going to church or temple."]

Test

Since we are a United Way-funded program, we are required to give you a test today. But this is a test that you can all pass, and it's actually a great way to add a high score to the grade book for this grading period. 

If you were not here all the days that I was, please make a note of that at the top of your test. I won't score questions on material that you missed.

Notice on the back that there is an essay section. This is a required part of the test. But you don't have to write a real essay. You have to write only 3 sentences. Tell me 3 things you learned this week, or, if you didn't learn anything new, 3 things you heard this week.

If you need help with anything, just raise your hand. I will be happy to talk through any question with you.
