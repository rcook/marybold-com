 Sex Abuse Statistics

The following information is not typically shared with students in the classroom:

Grubin and Wingate (1996) warn that statistics on sex abuse, especially, may be unreliable. Many abuses go unreported. Some reports go uninvestigated. 

Across the 1990s, reports of sex abuse decreased, but researchers do not conclude that there was less activity. That is not known. It is possible that reports have gone down as litigation for false accusations have gone up. Or it is possible that sex crimes decreased across the nation just as all crimes decreased in most cities during the 1990s. (U.S. government statistics showed a decline in physical abuse, also, in the late 1990s.)

Some therapists rely on a statistic that a sex offender has as many as 50 victims in his lifetime. But statistics on recidivism (re-arrest) are not so clear. As Grubin and Wingate (1996) report, the recidivism rate ranges from about 20% (non-violent) to 30% (rapists) for sex offenders. However, caveats are usually offered about such statistics: perhaps the sex offender has had multiple victims before being arrested, perhaps the offender learns how to avoid detection following a single arrest, perhaps law enforcement records are not well enough coordinated to reflect the number of offenders who move to a new location following a first arrest.

In short, as we have learned more about sex offenders in the past decade, our statistical information has not necessarily become any more reliable.
