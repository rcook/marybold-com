 Divorce

Divorce in America has been "relatively" stable since 1980. The steep increase in the divorce rate occurred between 1965 and 1980. Those years were marked by three fast-spreading ideas:

  • women can accomplish birth control with 98% effectiveness (following the introduction of "the pill" in 1964)
  • divorce can be mutual and non-shaming (following the introduction of "no-fault divorce" in 1969)
  • wives can ease family financial burdens by entering the paid work force (following the oil crunch and related economic factors in the 1970s)

While no single event made divorce more accepted in American society, the cumulative effect of these and other societal conditions changed how Americans viewed divorce. In retrospect, the woman's movement can be seen to have crested in the 1970s and, in fact, that decade is now referred to as the second wave of feminism. (The first wave was early in the 20th century and is marked by the 1920 passage of women's right to vote, although efforts leading to that change began in the mid-1850s.)

Since 1980, other demographic changes have "shored up" the liberal attitude toward divorce. For example, the median marrying ages of both men and woman have increased steadily. Women's enrollment in college and graduate school has steadily increased (and in 1998 women started earning more than half of the country's Master's degrees) making them more independent economically and more competitive for high salaries in the job market. In the 1990s, especially, economic well-being produced what it usually does: more open attitudes toward others' lifestyles and more optimistic reports of our own lives.

Family demographics have also changed dramatically. Today, most children know someone who is divorced—and it may be their own parent. These statistics offer perspective (Schertz, 2001):

  • While the majority of individuals over the age of thirty grew-up in a "traditional" or nuclear family, 74% of today's households are single parent or blended families.

  • Trends suggest that by about 2005, 50% of American families with children under the age of 18 will be blended families.

  • 80% of the children under the age of 15 will live some portion of their lives in either single parent or blended families.

Widespread occurrence of divorce and blended families serves to normalize divorce and to continue the high level of acceptance in today's society.

The level of divorce is high compared to the rest of the world. This prompts an important question: could the U.S. really have changed so much in such a short period of time that the nation would be so very different from other countries? Taking a look at the divorce question from a historical perspective forces us to admit that the sudden rise in divorce may not have been so sudden, after all.

Historically, Americans have always tolerated separation and short marriages. To start with, consider the average length of marriage in colonial America: about a dozen years (Coontz, 1992). "You grew up, got married, had some kids, and you died." Even by 1900, life expectancy was a short 47 years. With the median marrying age for men at that time being 26, the length of marriage was not even double that of colonial times.

What is not well known is that Americans had a relatively high divorce rate in the "olden days" when compared to the rest of the world. And it had an even higher desertion rate (Riley, 1991). Ending a marriage was easily done: a husband (typically, it was the male spouse) simply packed up and left. By moving to a new territory, he could start over, perhaps even marry again, without ever legally divorcing his wife and without providing for support of any children left behind. Abandoned wives frequently posted public notices about their missing husbands, to little effect.

Is desertion/separation/divorce an American tradition? Some historians believe that is the case (Riley, 1991). Outside of Native American populations, early Americans are described variously as adventuresome, risk-taking, pioneer, individualistic, rebellious, and even criminal. Early America was not founded by rule followers. 

The following information is not typically shared with students in the classroom:

Researchers in the family field debate the "true" question of divorce and its effect on children. One side, sometimes referred to as the "decline theory orientation," maintains that divorce reflects the increasingly self-centered and short-sighted nature of adults. In short, such theorists believe the family is "in decline" and suffering due to changes in family structure. The wide acceptance of single parenthood is the root of the problem, regardless of whether the single status is the result of divorce or of never having married. While theorists in this camp usually call for a return to two-parent homes, this is almost always in reference to two biological or adoptive parents (the "original" parents) and not dual parenting that arises from re-marriage.

The other side of the debate is sometimes referred to as "normative-adaptive." Theorists in this camp consider divorce a societal convention that reflects economic and lifestyle trends. In terms of effects of divorce, these theorists insist that the health of the family depends on how the people in it are getting along, not the number or structure of family membership. In other words, poor functioning on the part of a child whose parents are divorced actually is tied to the family functioning years before the divorce took place. For children in high-conflict homes, the divorce may even be a positive resolution.
