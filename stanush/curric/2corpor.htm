 Corporal punishment, spanking

The Parenting Center takes an unequivocal stand on spanking: it is never recommended. There are too many other ways to deal with children's misbehavior to justify spanking.

[Bold's second comment, when asked: It's a risk. You can't guarantee that you'll never be so angry that you hit too hard. You can't guarantee that your slap won't make the kid lose his balance and end up on the brick fireplace with a gash in his forehead. Or brain damage from a fall. It's a risk that I'm not willing to take.

Bold's third comment, when pressed: When I was a new mom, I watched a debate on spanking on the Phil Donahue show. That was a TV talk show like Oprah, except that Phil is an old white guy. The debate was raging: the experts said Never Spank and the audience said Are You Going to Let the 2-year-old Run In Front of a Truck? (Always an odd argument for spanking!) The debate was getting nowhere. But I noticed something. Every time an older woman spoke, the comment was something like: "If I had it to do over, I wouldn't spank. It wasn't necessary and it just caused more problems." That comment was made by several older women. Neither Phil nor his experts noticed. But I did. Many times I make a choice without "proof." I can listen to other people's experience and I can hear them say that I maybe I need to avoid regrets later. That's what I did and I'm glad I did.]

The following information is not typically shared with students in the classroom:

Americans clearly consider spanking a reasonable child-rearing technique. This is not the thinking around the world, but Americans have not routinely compared their techniques with others'. For example, in Sweden, corporal punishment is literally against the law. In the U.S., such punishment is only recently controlled in school settings.

In working with families on this topic, we may find high resistance to using alternatives to spanking. Many people are defensive about the use of spanking, even if they did not like to be spanked when they were children. Depending on the venue, family and parenting professionals may need to limit debate in order to avoid barriers to other discussions.

This is the case for PEPS Educators in the classroom. For many adolescents, the issue of spanking is not an important theme, anyway. A classroom debate may not represent the issue at all, but instead be an exercise in challenging an adult. If this is so, the PEPS Educator has already lost as soon as the debate begins.

Even if students are genuinely interested in "defending corporal punishment," they are likely defending a piece of personal identity. ("My dad hit me, but he's my dad and I love him. Don't tell me he was doing something bad.") If the PEPS Educator pursues the argument, Dad will only look worse and student will only be more offended.
