 Hard Times

Hard times, which can actually refer to any one of numerous adverse conditions, can happen to anyone. How well people bounce back can be related to many possible factors. Some of those factors are:

  • resources the person or the family has at the ready (this can mean money, time, a strong social network, and so forth)
  • experience with minor setbacks in the past that may have actually prepared them for dealing with future setbacks
  • personal outlook (positive or negative, may be tied to inborn temperament)
  • number of other problems going on at the same time

If there is not a pile-up of hard times, the individual or family can learn from the worst of problems and "re-organize" to a level of functioning higher than their pre-problem level. Thus, the resilient person sometimes credits the hard times for growth or increased skills.

The following information is not typically shared with students in the classroom:

Because poor people typically have fewer resources with which to respond to adversity, we have often directed research to see how poverty and resiliency are linked. Virtually all studies conclude that low socioeconomic status (SES) is tied to higher risk of not bouncing back and even to maladjustment. 

Other linkages backed by research (Tiet, et al., 1998):

Adverse life events are related to maladjustment.

Well-adjusted adolescents have lower levels of adverse life events.

Resilient youth live in higher functioning families and receive more guidance and supervision.

Good adjustment is related to living with two biological parents, good marital relations between parents, higher SES, closer parental monitoring, higher family functioning, better physical health, higher child IQ.

Good adjustment in youth is also related to locus of control.

Note that the research results are almost always couched in terms of "relationship" and "associated with." Simply put, researchers cannot prove which comes first, A or B.
