Introduction to Child Abuse

Transparency 4a

When we discuss child maltreatment, or abuse, we usually divide the topic into 3 types. Briefly, the definitions are: 

  • emotional abuse, damages the child's self-esteem

  • physical abuse, physical injury that is not caused by a normal childhood accident

  • sexual abuse, any unwanted sexual activity.

Transparency 4b

I want to give you an overview of factors that are often seen when abuse occurs. This doesn't mean that one factor always indicates that a child is being abused. Abuse can occur when none of these factors is present. But the professionals look for patterns such as these:

Stress or anxiety is not being managed. That could be tied to a stress like losing a job, or a parent carrying around a lot of anxiety all the time for no apparent reason.

Marital conflict, especially if it's high-level conflict, may be a factor.

Unrealistic expectations of a child can lead to abuse. Toilet training is a prime example in this country. Unfortunately, some parents think that a child must be trained by age 2, and they're willing to beat the child into performing. But normal child development predicts that a child won't be completely trained under about age 3½. 

Complete training means getting through the night dry, no diaper at all, and virtually no accidents. No pull-ups, either!

There are a few 2-year-olds out there who can pull that off, but not the majority. And it doesn't matter that people say all the cousins were trained before age 2, because it's amazing how many people re-write history and conveniently forget to mention the kid who went to nursery school at 3 in diapers.

Once parents understand what kids can and cannot do at certain ages, the pressure is off. Parents then have realistic expectations for their children's behavior, and the risk of abuse is much lower.

Substance abuse — and by that I mean using of any kind: drugs, alcohol, whatever — is often a factor in child abuse. The parent is not "present" psychologically (Boss, 1999) and that represents a loss for the child. It can also mean that the parent is more likely to strike out at the child, simply because the parent is not in control.

Low self-esteem of parents is harder to see. A parent may strike out at a child from a fatalistic position. "My life is crummy. I'm crummy. You must be crummy, too." So, the child is abused because the parent doesn't like himself or herself.

Role reversal with a child is another factor. This is the classic example: a woman beats her 5-year-old son because he didn't get her up to get to work on time. The child is expected to be the adult, and is punished when he doesn't get it right.

Social isolation is sometimes a factor. This doesn't mean that if a family lives 5 miles from the closest neighbor, that they abuse their kids. In fact, geography doesn't have anything to do with it. The isolation may be from neighbors just over the fence, or next door in an apartment. Social isolation means that the kids are prevented from playing at other people's houses. They are not allowed outside as much as other kids in the neighborhood. Maybe they never have anyone visit. Maybe they are never sent to school. Certainly, there are different levels of social interaction, and some families are more private, or don't allow sleep-overs, for example. But complete social isolation is not usual.

A history of abuse may be present in some cases, but we don't want to make the mistake of assuming that if a kid is abused, he or she will grow up to be an abuser. Many abused children grow up and make a conscious decision not to abuse their own kids. In fact, we know that about 1/3 of abused kids are already taking good care of themselves by the time they're adolescents. They figure out what's happening to them and they find ways to better their lives.

This doesn't mean that we won't continue to help kids who are being abused. After all, most abused kids still need help. So we still must provide counseling or whatever other help those children need. But it's amazing that as many as a third of the kids bounce back on their own. We need to give those kids more credit for what they manage to do for themselves.

Transparency 4f

Types of Sexual Abuse

Sexual abuse in any unwanted sexual activity. That's the "safest," "most protective" definition we can use. But there are different types of sexual abuse.

Rape is well understood and I will assume that you don't need an explanation of that.

Fondling refers to touching the body, and usually touching genitalia. Note that fondling can be the adult touching the child, or the child touching the adult.

Exhibitionism — do you know what that is? We make jokes about it in this culture. We laugh about the "dirty old man in the raincoat" flashing his nudity by opening the coat. It's not always the raincoat scenario, of course, but that's usually what the jokes are about. Now, I'd guess that most people in this room would not feel threatened by a flasher in the park, at least not if you were with a group of friends and it was broad daylight. But under certain conditions, any one of us could be rattled by an exhibitionist. And consider how scary it is to little kids, or to people more fragile than you. It does rank as a form of sexual abuse.

Voyeurism — another source of jokes in American culture. Are you familiar with this term? It means "peeping tom" or the person with binoculars watching people undress, or anything else they think they're doing in private. We make jokes about it, but it doesn't feel funny if you're on the receiving end of this behavior.

Child pornography — I don't mean by this just showing pornography to a child, which in itself is an act of both sexual abuse and emotional abuse. I also mean using a child as the subject of pornography. This is beyond children's understanding; pornography exploits children. 

Not the Victim's Fault

The Parenting Center stresses a key point about sexual abuse: it is never the victim's fault. This is a hard concept for a lot of victims of sex abuse. They may feel very responsible. And frequently the perpetrator takes advantage of this feeling, making the child feel guilty and secretive.

Children are naturally egocentric. They think the world revolves around them. Your basic 4-year-old looks over the play yard and assumes that the purpose of those other kids' existence is to be his playmates. He doesn't see them as individuals like himself. He considers only his own interpretation of what happens. Understanding that other people have independent thoughts is called theory of mind. We think that the earliest children can grasp that is about age 5. That means that plenty of kids are still struggling with the concept in elementary school. 

This self-centeredness sets kids up for guilt and shame not only for their own actions, but for others', too. The sexual abuser adds to it, "You'd better not tell anyone because then there'd be trouble." The egocentric child assumes that he or she is central to everything. So, they keep the secret all too often.

Transparency 4g

I want to tell you what we know about sexual abuse.

[Students may read ahead on the transparency and interrupt with a question. If possible, respond immediately to the question. "Yes, you're drawn to something that is compelling for all of us. I'll come back to my list later. Let's talk about X now.]

Victims — I cannot promise you that these figures are true: the rate of victims is perhaps 1 in 4 girls and 1 in 6 boys. I have also seen statistics of 1 in 5 girls and 1 in 10 boys. Why the uncertainty? First of all, researchers define "sexual abuse" differently. Here, we define it broadly, including all the forms we've talked about. Second, sexual abuse may be under-reported. The number of reports went down in the 1990s. Researchers don't know if that's because there really is less abuse, or because lawsuits are making people hesitate before turning in someone. Third, we are pretty sure that boys do not report all the abuse that they receive. So, no one knows the true extent of sexual abuse. What I can tell you is that even "uncertain" statistics are better than what we used to have — no statistics. When I was a child, no one discussed sexual abuse. 

[Option: Depending on your own age and experience, additional comment might include: My generation brought sexual abuse out in the open. I'm proud of that. It took a lot of courage for people to speak up and call for protection of children. But my generation has not done a good job of figuring out what to do with the perpetrators. We jail some. We medicate some. We put them in therapy. We put them in half-way houses. We put their names on the Internet. What we have not been able to do is stop them from repeating. The known recidivism rate is 20% to 30% (Grubin & Wingate, 1996) and we don't know how many offenders repeat and don't get caught. This is work for your generation. But don't think it's going to be easy. This is tough. Sexual predators often genuinely believe that they mean children no harm. It's a fact that we have not been able to reach many of them, and yet plenty of smart people have tried. Maybe it takes a full generation to move just one step toward a solution. My generation got the issue out of the closet. Yours may be the one to really change the character of treatment.]

Perpetrators — The great majority are male. Certainly there have been cases of female sex abusers, but their numbers are small. There appears to be a pattern in abusers; most were abused when they were younger.

95% of Abusers —  … are known to the child. This means that the sexual abuser is usually Dad, or Grandpa, or the camp counselor, or the fellow who lives down the street. Sex offenders almost always know their victims well and they typically work hard at building a close relationship with the child and even the child's family. 

Passive Parents — There may be a passive parent who knows or "tries not to know" what is going on. This may be a mother who is covering up abuse of her own. Or maybe it's a mom who realizes that if the sexual abuse comes out in the open, she will lose the family breadwinner. Or the family may break apart. 

Youngest Case on Record — The youngest age we know of was a 2-week-old girl. This was a case of penetration and she died. [Students may ask what "penetration" is. A simple answer is best: "an adult penis is inserted in the baby's vagina. Tissue is torn; repair may not be possible. In the case of this infant, no repair was possible." Students may be silent for some time as they accommodate this information; most speakers will want to proceed with the talk quietly and slowly until students are ready to join discussion again.]

Abuse can extend beyond childhood — Sometimes we get impatient with an older victim and we ask "Why didn't she just a, b, c." One of the toughest things about sexual abuse is understanding how helpless the victim feels. So, our logic cannot apply to all situations.
