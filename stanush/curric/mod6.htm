Introduction to Change

Transparency 6a

A basic issue for any family is how they handle change. The greatest challenge for families is the entry or exit of family members. 

An entry could be a baby being born. Sounds like a happy event — but it's one of the most stressful events for a family. An exit might be a death, sudden or expected. It might be a graduation that makes someone move away from the family home. 

Other changes? Maybe it's a single parent's romantic partner. Maybe it's a grandparent moving in. What else?

How a family reacts to change may tell us how they handle stress of all kinds.

Transparency 6b

Those of you who are headed to business school will study innovation models applied to corporations undergoing change. Change can be tracked in terms of acceptance and resistance cycles (McFarland, Senn, & Childress, 1994). 

In the model here, we look for what makes the difference between how people react to change. Basically, an increase of information reduces resistance and channels energy to the change cycle. 

Think about a business example: it's time to break up the corporation into smaller units. Does management make all the decisions and announce on a Friday that on Monday morning all employees will report to new offices in different buildings around town? Experience has shown that the employees will not respond well. They won't be great employees and the company may actually lose a lot of money while the workers make a slow adjustment to the changes.

That's why so many businesses today share information freely with their employees. Change is announced far in advance so that employees can make suggestions and be part of the change-over. If the workers help plan the change, they are not as resistant to it. 

Let's apply this to families. If a change is coming, maybe the accordion effect of step-siblings coming and going during summer vacation, the more information everyone has, the better. And the more the kids are invited to participate in planning, the better. So, we can sum up the change model in two words: communication and participation.

Obviously, all people don't respond to change in the same way. You may know someone with a personality that just doesn't tolerate change. Maybe no amount of information will help that person make a change. Maybe only a lot of adjustment time will help. But most of us do respond to the need to change as long as we are given lots of information and given the chance to participate in making the changes.

Family beliefs about change can be unique, just as individuals' beliefs are. The Green Family may think that change is great and maybe they move every 3 years just to have the variety of living many places. The Red Family may think that moving from the family home is a tragedy. For a military family, moving may be so routine they don't worry about the changes they have to make. We can't assume that families treat change the same way, just as we can't assume that individuals react to it the same way.
