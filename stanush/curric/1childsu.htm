 Child Support

Payment of child support remains one of the thorniest issues surrounding divorce and remarriage. Much emotion is attached to the issue and child support is often used as a "club" both in divorce settlement negotiations and in long-term relations among family members.

Although some women do pay child support to ex-husbands, the vast majority of payments are in the other direction, from men to ex-wives. Historically, women have not been good collectors of payments. Regular and on-time payments are almost always the doing of the father (on his own) or the doing of the court (by requiring payment be made and providing penalty for nonpayment). The number of non-paying fathers is unknown, but federal programs report that their tracking systems have increased the number of paying fathers by 32% (reported in 2000).

The term "dead-beat dad" refers to fathers who do not make child support payments. Across the 1990s, some states took action to trace the whereabouts and financial holdings of such fathers. In Texas, the Attorney General's office runs an aggressive program that includes posting photographs and detailed information on the Internet about "Evaders." The program website is http://www.oag.state.tx.us/child/mainchil.htm .

The following information is not typically shared with students in the classroom:

Adolescents' views about child support may be influenced heavily by their own experience or by a friends' experience. Rarely will adolescents have a "big picture" of child support as a societal issue. As an issue, child support is outside the concern of the PEPS curriculum; however, as a common concern in blended families today, it may be mentioned in class.

Because of families' emotional view of this topic, it is almost always discussed with opinion, not neutrally. Negative statements are common: fathers try to weasel out of paying; parents play games with the child support payments; if a support agreement fails to address college tuition, it may be a voluntary payment, not a required one.

Child support may be a topic covered in "divorce education," lectures that divorcing parents are required to attend. However, support is rarely otherwise discussed in detail outside of lawyers' offices. It is often seen as a negotiable item in divorce settlements and this view may extend to family discussions. 

In the family field, considerable debate surrounds the efficacy of "divorce education" in terms of actually changing parents' behavior following divorce. Wallace Goddard (2000) has suggested that court-directed curricula fail to change behavior, but that sometimes the goal is to simply make parents feel guilty about their post-divorce behavior and in that way suppress some amount of destructive behavior.
