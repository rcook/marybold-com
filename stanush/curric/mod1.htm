Family Composition: What Is a Family?

Transparency 1b

So, we start with our legal definitions. The Census Bureau calls a family "a group of two persons or more (one of whom is a householder) related by birth, marriage or adoption and residing together" (U.S. Bureau of the Census, 1998). And "householder" refers to whoever owns or rents the home (U.S. Bureau of the Census, 2001).

[OPTION: On the inside of your name card, write your own definition of family. What does that word mean to you?]

We don't have to live by the Census Bureau definition here in the classroom, but it's a good starting point for discussion, so let's do what we'd do in a college classroom: let's de-construct the definition.

Colored strings

I need 2 volunteers who are next to each other. Doesn't matter if we have a man and woman, or two men, two women, anyone can do this and you don't even have to stand up. Thank you — these 2 will serve as our first family. [Any configuration works as students volunteer; female students are frequently the first volunteers, though.] A woman and her daughter; who wants to be the parent? Thank you. Let's connect these two with a pink string.

I've named these two a family of mother and daughter. So, do they meet the U.S. Census Bureau definition of family? Yes. So, what about other people in their lives who might be family?

Ah, the father. OK, where's a man nearby? Thank you. We'll make this father live in another home, so he's not part of the family-by-definition, right? Another string running from the man to — to whom? Who will hold the other end of the string? OK. The daughter. You see that as the family connection?

Well, does the father count as family by the legal definition? No. Out of household, out of family. But does this daughter call him family? Sure. Now, you wanted the daughter to hold the string, but I'm going to insist that this father and mother have a connection, even though they are not living together. Let me offer a white string to these parents. Will you grant me that it could be a pink string? Pink for family? 

The following information is not typically shared with students in the classroom: Ex-spouses are examples of "latent relationships" that may be re-activated at a later point in time (Riley, 1983). As divorce and re-marriage continue to describe a large proportion of American couples, there is a growing matrix of latent relationships that affects not just the once-married couple but also all the in-laws, any children from the marriage, and also children created in re-marriages. A relationship may appear to end, but because of the matrix it has not really been severed. More likely, it is latent or lying dormant. It may be re-activated when family is "convened" for a wedding or a funeral. It may be re-activated when someone needs something: an ex-spouse may be called on for care, support, or finances in case of grave illness or financial emergency.

OK. You're telling me this couple is an ex-couple and they don't consider themselves family. But you'll grant me that there's still a connection? My white string states that these people are still connected — probably for life.

What other family might our mom-daughter dyad call their family? OK — grandparents. Who's old in this room? Who's willing to be grandparents to this girl? Thank you. We have a grandmother here and a grandfather there. How about a long gold string going to the granddaughter?

What about other grandparents? OK, more gold string from these other grandparents to the granddaughter, right? So, lots of string emanating from her position. What about the parents' relationships with these grandparents? 

Who else? OK. An aunt and uncle and 2 cousins will do. More gold string from the daughter — running it to whom?

Let me suggest another extended family member — Auntie Gracie. She's not  actually a relative, but she lives with the aunt and uncle, and everyone considers her family. She is "fictive kin." Research has shown that "family" refers to the people we're in contact with — who we communicate with, who we celebrate with. Most of us call those people family, regardless of whether we're blood relatives (Jorgenson, 1989). So, the gold string to our daughter over there must also be held by Auntie Gracie.

Now, we've moved across multiple households, and we're still claiming family with a lot of string, but our government is not calling this family.

Well, historically, people have always defined family the way they've wanted to. Our usual term for the small unit — mom and daughter over there — is nuclear family. And the extensions out to the other relatives — that's extended family. 

But I want to point out that the daughter in the nuclear family has two homes, really. She spends time at Dad's place, too. So, this is actually a bi-nuclear family. The English majors in the room will want us to be specific: the nucleus is the center part, but this family has two nucleuses or two nuclei. So, the "bi" in bi-nuclear means there are two centers of growth.

If we move some extended family into the same house as our nuclear family, they become "legal" family by definition, but these people already considered themselves family. So, our legal definitions are pretty narrow and that's typical of most definitions. 

Anyone defining family is probably doing it for a narrow purpose. On a form, "family" almost always means "just who's living in the home." Sometimes the name on the form is very narrow: who's paying for this kid's braces? 

Most of us do not define family when we talk about our family members. Most of us just accept that we all know what the word means, but we also know that it means something different to each of us (Trost, 1990). I have a friend who lives just with her cat. Now, the Census Bureau doesn't call that a family, but my friend does. 

Let's go back to our mom-daughter nuclear family and change that composition. Let's marry mom off to a man who has a son. I'd like to start some green string — to where? I need two men  who are nearby — thank you. Green is now representing "blended" family members. If we use the daughter as our reference point again, who are we calling family at this point? Do we still need some pink string to signify the nuclear family members of dad and son before they blended with mom and daughter?

What other string do we need for the folks over in this corner? [Continue until every student is linked.] 

You folks feeling  pretty complicated yet? Families are becoming increasingly different and the relationships are increasingly complex (Dressel, 1996).

Images of Family

Transparency 1d

Television hasn't always given us such a wide variety of family types. About mid-century in the 1950s and 1960s, the typical image of the American family was a dad who went off to work, a mom who stayed home, and a couple of kids who were born after that mom and dad's first and only marriage. 

But that was only an image! The majority of American families never actually matched that image. In 1940, 41% of Americans looked like that. In 1963, the figure was 43% (Hernandez, 1993). And those were the top years! So, the image never matched even half of American families.

We often have images in our culture that don't reflect the way things really were. For example, most people believe that 100 years ago, the "extended family" meant that grandparents lived with the parents and children. The assumption is that children received support from their grandparents and that today's children don't get the benefits of extended family.

But 100 years ago, most kids didn't know a grandparent, or if they did it was only for about 10 years (Bold, 2001; Cherlin & Furstenberg, 1986; Hagestad, 1988).

Just looking at the shape of families then and now, we can see that families have changed a lot, and kids today are much more likely to be connected to their grandparents. My old-days picture shows a pyramid: a few old people at the top, lots of kids at the bottom. My now-days picture shows a different population shape, more of a block with almost as many old people in the society as there are kids.

Transparency 1e

That brings us to my picture of the beanpole family composition. More and more, this is how families are built (Longino & Earle, 1996; Giarrusso, Silverstein, & Bengtson, 1996). Youngest generation at the bottom would be you folks. Next up is the generation that raised you. Next up is what I'd call "grandparent generation." Show of hands, please: how many people in this room have a living grandparent? How about 2 grandparents alive? 3? 4? More? With blended families today, plenty of folks have 6 and 8 grandparents (Henry & Plunkett, 1996). More than 10 years ago, researchers figured that 1/3 of grandparents were also step-grandparents (Longino & Earle, 1996). It's higher now, but I don't know the statistic.

Can we keep going up the beanpole? Anyone here with a great-grandparent alive? Yes? Then your family spans 4 generations. Not many families have ever done that before. I'll go ahead and ask, anyone here have 5 generations living? Medical researchers tell us that will be the next trend, as more and more people live past 100.
