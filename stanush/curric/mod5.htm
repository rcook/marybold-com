What Happens Next:
That's how the cookie crumbles …

Transparency 5a

We've talked about big issues, like sexual abuse, coming out in the open. Now, let's consider some of the things that might happen next. 

Think back to the Ann Landers letter from D.R., the 13-year-old girl who's afraid of getting Gary in trouble.

Let's say D.R. tells her mother what's going on. Then what?

Yes, Gary may be kicked out of the house.

Or, yes, he may be arrested.

Good point. Mom may tell D.R. to stop lying. So, she may want to deny what's going on.

Yes. D.R. may be kicked out of the house. And what? Sent to grandma's to live?

You're right: someone might get hurt.

No matter what happens next, it's going to be rough, isn't it? Some families fall apart. That cookie may crumble. The fear of the family falling apart keeps some families living with the abuse or keeping the secret of past abuse.

Maybe the abuser is the primary source of money for the family. Or maybe the abuser has threatened to do something worse if anyone tells. An abuser may say, "I'll kill you if you tell anyone." The victim doesn't think logically: if the abuser is sent to prison, killing won't be possible. On the other hand, maybe the victim is afraid the abuser won't be sent to prison. The victim sees telling as being a big risk.

[Optional video: Eva's Story. If you used the video, you may want to revisit the "aftermath" for Eva's family, pointing out that Eva spoke up to save her sister. We don't know who the abuser would have chosen for the next victim. Perhaps a grandchild, someday.]

As a PEPS Educator, I still recommend that people tell the secret. The damage that comes with the cookie crumbling is likely to be less than the damage that comes with keeping the secret. But I won't dismiss the importance of a break-up of the family. The family may have a lot of heartbreak ahead of them. But there's another way to look at it: the whole family can carry the burden of the problem instead of one little child carrying it alone.

From society's point of view, we want abuse uncovered. Most researchers believe that sex offenses are under-reported (Grubin & Wingate, 1996) and this may skew all the statistics we have about sex abuse. In general, we wonder about all abuse statistics. We do have more abuse reported today than 30 years ago, but that does not mean there is more abuse. More likely, it means that we have made reporting easier and more acceptable.
