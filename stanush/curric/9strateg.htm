 Strategies

Steve and Sylvia Wolin (2001) suggest use of a "vocabulary" that identifies resiliencies that can be incorporated in the daily life of at-risk youth:

  • Insight — asking tough questions and giving honest answers.
  • Independence — distancing emotionally and physically from the sources of trouble in one's life.
  • Relationships — making fulfilling connections to other people.
  • Initiative — taking charge of problems.
  • Creativity — using imagination and expressing oneself in art forms.
  • Humor — finding the comic in the tragic.
  • Morality — acting on the basis of an informed conscience. 

The following information is not typically shared with students in the classroom:

Much of the "teaching" of resilience acknowledges that some young people will have to distance themselves from destructive family members in order to move toward self-care. Especially in school settings, this is a message that is potentially risky. The Wolins (and many others who work in the resilience field) come from therapy backgrounds in which self-efficacy by clients is a goal. Outside of the therapy world, advisors of distancing from family are not well tolerated. Thus, the strategies listed above (such as "taking charge") may be seen as insubordination in some homes. A curriculum promoting such strategies must be well-grounded and fully supported.
