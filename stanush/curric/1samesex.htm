 Same Sex Partners

Recognition of same-sex partners raising children is new. Just in the past couple of decades have same-sex parents made their sexual orientation known as they raised children in their homes.

Families with same-sex partners face challenges in this society, and sometimes the children face special challenge from peers at school. In spite of these difficulties, research on the children's development has come in with one major observation: these kids are developing normally (Stacy, 1998).

We can count on kids being kids, regardless of their parents' sexual orientation. Families with kids go to MacDonald's, and the soccer field, and G-rated movies. There's a span of years, there, when family focus just isn't on parents. It's on kid activities.

Too, the children in these families do not adopt a sexual orientation based on their parents' model. The rate of homosexual lifestyle is the same for children from every other kind of family. This is interesting, because we have wondered for a long time now about how important "gender models" are to young children, and to older children, too.

The one research view that finds a difference is that children from same-sex-parent homes may be quicker to help classmates stand up to school bullies. That research result may reflect just some kids in one town, so we're wise not to assume that it describes all children in same-sex-parent homes. But we can imagine that these children may indeed learn empathy at an early age since they may be the target of discrimination themselves.

The following information is not typically shared with students in the classroom:

It is possible that the research on normal development of children in same-sex-parent homes is a reflection of a "cream of the crop" set of parents. Homosexuals deciding to openly parent today find barriers that they must first commit to facing and then commit to overcoming. Thus, the decision to parent is a serious one that is likely to be made only after considerable soul-searching. The current apparent health of same-sex-parent families may reflect superior parenting commitment and skills on the part of a relatively small group of people.

Too, the normal development of boys in homes without males in residence may be a function of having "cream of the crop" moms. Lesbians committed to parenting a son well may be taking extra effort to include men in their son's life. So, just because a man does not live with the boy does not mean that the boy is not in frequent contact with men.

If acceptance of same-sex parenting continues to grow, the population of same-sex parents will surely grow in number and become more diverse. We may see mediocre and poor parents in the next generation, which may result in child profiles that are not as positive as today's.

Research into lesbian parents' fears (Hare, 1994; Siegenthaler, 1999) reveal that highest on the list is that their children may be ostracized at school or in some way discriminated against or embarrassed. Lesbian parents overwhelmingly give credit to their families of origin for supporting their parenting efforts.
