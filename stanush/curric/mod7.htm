Introduction to Emotional Abuse

Transparency 7a

All of these words have meaning when we discuss emotional abuse: self-esteem, self-concept, nurturing, rejecting, ignoring.

We use the words to help define emotional abuse, with the understanding that definitions are subjective. We started the week with a Census Bureau definition of family and saw that people define family much more broadly. Abuse is defined differently by different families, and it is viewed differently in different communities.

So, our challenge is to find a description that can cross communities, cross cultures, and even cross families. This is harder to do with emotional abuse than with physical or sexual abuse. In those forms, abuse is more obvious: there are observable behaviors and consequences. Emotional abuse may be invisible to the outsider. It can even be invisible to the abuser!

Emotional abuse attacks the victim's self-esteem or self-concept. Depending on the personality of the victim, the damage may be kept "under wraps." Unfortunately, the damage is often long-term. 

Let's put it in terms of a preschooler: the child believes everything big people say. If a parent says, "You're stupid," the child does what? Yes, takes on that identification. I don't know what to do with the dog leash because I'm stupid. The child will make the next connection on his own: I don't know how to read because I'm stupid. I don't know how to make friends because I'm stupid. The connection can extend to every aspect of the child's life, and it can last all the rest of the child's life.

Was the child ever stupid to begin with? Probably not! Most likely, someone called the child stupid when the child was simply acting like a child. Little kids don't know how to do lots of things. They are ignorant of the workings of the world. They literally see the world differently than we do. If they have helpful guides, they'll learn everything they need to. If they have impatient or abusive guides, they'll shut down emotionally and not learn their way around the world.

Preschool View of the World

Transparency 7b

Here's a sample of how a preschooler sees the world. If we pour 8 ounces of water into each of 2 containers, we know we have equal amounts of water. But the preschooler sees it differently: the tall glass must have more water because the water level is higher.

Even if we were to pour out the water and "prove" they were equal amounts, the child would still give the wrong answer when the liquids were poured back into the 2 different containers.

Eventually, the child is able to grasp that the volume is "conserved" regardless of shape. But at a young age, this is simply not understood.

[Optional explanation: This is the theory of conservation as explain by the Swiss child developmentalist, Jean Piaget. When similar "conservation tasks" are presented to children under the age of 6 or 7, the children are typically unable to make the correct choice. Piaget described their thinking as pre-operational. The next stage of thinking (operational) is entered by most children by age 7 and characterizes what most of us think of in terms of elementary school learning ability. Age 7 is also a typical time for children to grasp the concepts of time and money, abstractions that younger children may have in their vocabulary but without any real understanding.]

Young children cannot "reason" and figure out what's real and what's not. They live in something akin to a fantasy world. They may learn to say that something is "pretend," but you'll notice they can't define that word! 

[Optional story: In the early 1990s, researchers interviewed 4-year-olds on the east coast with a single question: "Is Big Bird real?" All the 4-year-olds answered "Yes" except for one little girl. She answered, "No, Big Bird is not real. That is just a costume. Inside is a regular size bird."]

Barney lives, Cookie Monster eats cookies, and Pokemon characters have individual personalities. The fact that children "believe" what we put before them helps explain why they are so vulnerable to emotional abuse.

If a parent calls a child a name — stupid or lazy or fool — children believe it. The child will accept a label, whether it is good or bad.

Forms of Emotional Abuse

Transparency 7c

Rejecting — Name-calling is called "rejecting." It also includes put-downs. I don't think any of us ever get old enough to not feel the sting of a rejection, but children are especially vulnerable. They don't know how to analyze the put-down and figure out that the other person is speaking out of anger.

Terrorizing — Another form of emotional abuse is "terrorizing." Again, the child's inability to reason makes her vulnerable. If a big person says, "Move one muscle and I'm going to break your fingers" the child cannot sort out what may or may not happen.

Ignoring — This entails taking care of the child's physical needs but withholding affection, smiles, and other forms of nurturing. An example of this form of abuse is the parent who chooses to ignore the child's birthday. When the child sees other kids at school bringing cupcakes on their birthday, the child may ask, "When is my birthday?" The ignoring parent will reply, "You don't have one."

Corrupting — Exposing a child to adult activities is abusive because the child has no way to process the information. This could mean exposing a child to pornography, or taking her to a drinking party, or including her in drug use. Of course, some of these involve physical risk, also. But even if they don't, they are emotionally abusive.

Isolating — Preventing a child from interacting with other children is called isolating. Even the child who lives in an apartment building can be "socially isolated," with neighbors literally just a few feet away. 

The Opposite of Emotional Abuse: Nurturance

Transparency 7d

Nurturing children means meeting their needs according to their developmental level. 

For babies, the developmental needs are physical care, attachment to a responsive caregiver, and brain stimulation. There is a genuine mix of physical care and emotional care that takes place in infancy. From the baby's perspective, the mix of care means that he can trust that his needs will be met (Erikson, 1997). If the trust is there, we usually say that infant attachment is successful. If babies don't attach to a "significant other," they grow up to be pretty miserable people (Harris, 1998).

Just since the mid-1990s, we have come to understand that nurturing babies also means giving them mental stimulation. Brain stimulation promotes the "best" possible brain growth at crucial times in a human's life. 

We have just recently established that there's a window of opportunity in the first 3 years of life to create the best brain environment for cognitive development (White, 2001). In fact, 95% of what we know in the field of brain research has been discovered just since 1995 (Conyers, 2000).

Infant Brain Research

Transparency 7e

Here's just a sampling of what we know.

At birth, a baby has about 50 trillion synapses or "connections" between brain cells. The number will multiply by about 20 times in the first 3 months of life. So, at 3 months of age, the baby has about 1,000 trillion.

The number of synapses continues to grow if the child continues to be stimulated by the world around him. Every game of peek-a-boo, every baby book, every smile the baby receives — these all give the infant brain something to respond to, and the response is literally growth of synapses.

Although we can keep learning our entire lives, and we certainly know that the physical structure of the brain continues to change, there are certain "windows of time" for best brain growth. We have been able to see the first 3 years of life as such a window.

We know that when we compare the number of synapses at 3 years with the number at adulthood, there is a big change: by adulthood, about half of the synapses have disappeared. 

"Reduction of synapses" is usually described as "pruning." It is perfectly normal. The brain will not use all of the synapses that have been created, so it "prunes" the un-needed ones. What we would to be able to say is that all babies and preschoolers make so many synapses at the start, and stimulation is "kept up" so that the natural pruning that takes place does not work to the disadvantage of the child.

We know that in cases of severe child abuse and neglect, not enough synapses are built in the first place. Then, when pruning takes place, the child may lose the potential ability to do something simply because she is not exercising particular brain cells in her young life. 

Back to the topic of "normal pruning." I like to quote Dr. Ron Fannin (2001), who is a developmentalist at Texas Woman's University. He likens the pruning of synapses to growing topiary at Disney World. The gardeners do just plant a bush and hope that it grows in the shape of Mickey Mouse ears. Rather, they grow a great, bushy bush — and then prune selectively to create the desired shape. 

Our brains are something like topiary! We produce many synapses and then prune away what is not needed or is not used.

Our new knowledge about infant brain development is paired with new knowledge of a special risk to infants: Shaken Baby Syndrome. You may have heard about this in the news or on a public service announcement. We're trying to get the word out to families about the real chance for brain damage or even death when a baby is shaken. Even "tossing" a baby in the air can cause brain damage; certainly, shaking a baby in anger is enough to cause damage. It is impossible to know how many children suffered brain injury as infants because they were shaken. It's something that they pay for the rest of their lives as they are unable to function as they would have.
