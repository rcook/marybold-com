Catalog Description:

FS 5123. Theories of the Family. Theoretical approaches to the family; the nature and functions of the family; its relationship to institutions, culture, and the individual. Three lecture hours a week. Credit: Three hours.

 

 

Course Objectives:

  1. Develop an understanding of the theoretical bases for family science.
  2. Apply major theoretical frameworks to specific research areas in the family field.
  3. Be responsive to the contextual issues that shape the origins and development of major theoretical frameworks.
  4. Critically evaluate family theories.

 

 

Course Requirements:

  1. Participation in class meetings. For doctoral students, participation will include speaking on specified topics and answering questions from the class. 10% of semester grade.
  2. Contribute to a Theory Timeline, created as a threaded discussion in Blackboard. Submit a total of five (5) timeline entries pertinent to one decade. Each entry should run 2 or 3 sentences, with enough detail to be meaningful. If your source is the Boss text, include a reference with page number like this: (Boss-200). Other sources should be written in APA format. Last date for submissions: October 11. Timeline submissions = 10% of semester grade.
  3. Actively participate on a theory team, communicating either through Blackboard or private e-mail. Keep Bold posted on progress of your team’s work. Family Therapy and Counseling majors may NOT sign up for Systems theory; Child Development majors may NOT sign up Family Development Theory or Life Course Theory; Family Studies majors may NOT sign up for Ecological Theory.
  4. As a theory team, host a Blackboard discussion for the week assigned to your theory. Utilize the documents on the 5123 CD-ROM v 1.75 as starting points for the discussion. You can assume that classmates have read about the theory with these documents in mind. As hosts, you can decide how you want class members to comment: perhaps by answering study questions you pose at the start of the week, perhaps by contributing to a daily log in which you stimulate comments on a specific issue, perhaps by participating in an online debate or skit playing assigned parts, perhaps by assigning a vocabulary quiz or activity, perhaps by writing a scavenger hunt for information. Do not be afraid to experiment with new methods. You may direct class members concerning number of comments, length of comments, dates for comments, etc. You will find the Boss text rich in information to discuss; consider including the pertinent Application in the Boss text as this section frequently points to alternative views about the theory. Theory host activity = 10% of semester grade.
  5. As a class member, actively participate in each week of discussion. (Note: your access to Blackboard is through an Internet browser. Thus, you can make your contributions from home, from a hotel, from an airport….) Late postings earn 50% credit. Participation = 10% of semester grade.
  6. Contribute to a web sitelet called Theory Vocabulary. Study the graduate student sitelets at http://twu.edu/~f_bold as examples. Submissions will be strictly limited to100-word (not including references) entries. Research and writing should be scrupulously clean. Cite at least one (1) reference per entry, and use the Boss text sparingly. You will work with the same team as for #4 above, but this theory will NOT match your theory team focus. As a writing team you will be responsible for editing each others’ references as well as entries. The team will share a grade on the editing of the reference lists. Doctoral students will be responsible for the assignment of vocabulary terms and also for the final product. Last date for submissions: November 1. Earlier submissions will be appreciated. Work on submissions = 10% of semester grade. Score on edited reference lists = 10% of semester grade.
  7. Synthesis Paper: access documents on electronic reserve for this class and select a number of articles to write on. (Article list is also posted in Blackboard; most articles will be accessible through Blagg-Huey online databases.) All papers must be submitted electronically, with strong preference for Microsoft Word. In grading, Bold will check two things first: word count and reference list. If the word count is too long or the reference list has more than 3 errors, the paper will be returned un-graded. Paper(s) = 10% of semester grade.
    1. If your desired grade in this course is a B, you may select as few as three (3) articles, with a paper word count between 250 and 300 words (not counting references). For a B, your due date is October 25.
    2. If your desired grade in this course is an A, you must select at least ten (10) articles. You are welcome to add more source material, as long as you meet the minimum number from this reading list. Your total word count must be between 1000 and 1250 words (not counting references). Master’s students may choose between writing a single paper of 1000 words or two papers for a combined word count of 1000 words (about 500 words each and splitting the ten references between the two papers). For an A, your due date is November 15.
  8. Mid-term exam and final exam. To earn a B in this course, each exam grade must be B- or better. To earn an A in this course, each exam grade must be B+ or better. The mid-term exam will consist of objective items. The final exam will consist of about 30 objective items and several essay questions in a mock comps setting. Mid-term exam is 10% of semester grade. Final exam is 20% of semester grade. Exams will be conducted online.

 

Policies and Procedures:

  1. Written work must be typed. The style manual for the American Psychological Association (APA), 5th ed., should be used for citations, references, and manuscript style.
  2. Written work should be turned in according to class schedule. Grade deductions will be made for late work. Due dates are firm regardless of server problems.
  3. Papers should be posted to your “individual group” space on BB.

Written work, whether in Blackboard or a paper or a test, must display an understanding of the topic or question. Your responses must “fit” the question. If you invent a new question or take your answer down an alternative path, I must assume that you do not know the answer to the question posed. For grading purposes, this would equate to turning in a blank sheet.
