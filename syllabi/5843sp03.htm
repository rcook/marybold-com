Course Description: 

 

Theories and techniques of program planning, instruction, and evaluation in family sciences settings; emphasis on needs assessment and curriculum development. Three lecture hours a week. Credit: Three hours.

Course Objectives:

  1. Compare theories of curriculum development and instruction.
  2. Determine concepts, objectives, content, and instructional strategies relevant to family and consumer sciences.
  3. Develop instructional techniques based on curriculum goals and learner characteristics, encompassing those associated with both andragogy and pedagogy.
  4. Demonstrate varied teaching and learning methods and technologies.
  5. Design appropriate needs assessment strategies.
  6. Design appropriate evaluation strategies.
  7. Evaluate educational and professional programs.
  8. Evaluate teaching and learning resources.
  9. Demonstrate curriculum planning, delivery, and evaluation.
  10. Family & Consumer Sciences Post-baccalaureate Certification students: Develop applications appropriate to the Pedagogy and Professional Responsibilities (8-12) Standards, Technology Applications Standards, FCS Standards, and other standards pertinent to certification.

 

Course Requirements:

 

1.      Portal Account. Create and use a TWU Pioneer Portal e-mail account for use in online courses. If your e-mail to Bold reflects a Portal account, you will be credited 5 points.

2.      Track 1 or Track 2. Select a track (1: Face to face [F2F] or 2: Online) for assignments. Report choice to Bold via e-mail by February 1 (earlier decisions welcomed).

3.      Self Introduction. Use the Personal Information and Personal Homepage functions of FS 5843 Blackboard to introduce yourself to the class. 5 points.

4.      Vocabulary Board. Contribute to the development of extended course materials (called Vocabulary Board) for use by all class members in Blackboard. 15 points.

5.      Resource Board. Contribute to the development of extended course materials (called Resource Board) for use by all class members in Blackboard. Resources on this board will typically be websites of value to curriculum designers and writers, facilitators, teachers, and evaluators; however, print materials may also be listed. 5 points.

6.      Curriculum Development. Develop and present a workshop or curriculum module designed for presentation in community. Set a presentation date for February, March, or April; notify Bold via e-mail by February 15. On the same day as that notification e-mail, submit to your “Individual Group File Exchange” (in Blackboard) a schedule of your work that provides for all of the below. While this schedule is amendable, it should reflect your best estimate of dates that you can meet. Curriculum + Materials (including electronic displays) = 15 points; Curriculum Delivery (evidenced by videotape in Track 1 or electronic files in Track 2) = 15 points; Evaluation = 5 points; Plus/Delta report = 10 points. Total project =  45 points.

6.1.   Track 1: arrange to present before a live audience, videotape the event, and submit the videotape to Bold.

6.2.   Track 2: create and deliver electronic materials in a forum that can be viewed by Bold; if you do not have a ready audience, set a schedule for members of this class to act as your audience. (Possible forums/media for your curriculum: FS 5843 Blackboard space, a website posted in your Venus server space provided to every student by TWU, server space free on the Internet through various commercial and non-profit companies, supported teaching activities created in a Quia account or similar Internet service (free accounts run for a month), your own e-mail account using attachments, HTML documents created with or without editor software, PDF files, RTF files, video files, audio files and other “universal platform” formats, permanent media storage such as CD-ROM.)

6.3.   Both Tracks:

6.3.1.     Document the full curriculum, with appropriate original copyright notice or attribution of sources.

6.3.2.     Create hand-outs or other materials for distribution to your audience.

6.3.3.     Design promotional material to publicize your workshop.

6.3.4.     Create and administer an evaluation tool (typically, to be completed by your audience, although other evaluation tools may be appropriate to your project).

6.3.5.     Summarize the project in a Plus/Delta report to Bold within 5 days of your workshop. (Track 2 summaries are due within 5 days of the administration of the evaluation tool.)

7.      Application of Hitch & Youatt. Participate in review of the common text (Hitch & Youatt), using your own workshop as application of the concepts. This will require a scan of the entire text and then “placing dibs” on parts of the book (minimum of 5 reports per person; each report should be a minimum of 100 words) that are well suited to your teaching style or your workshop topic or your audience’s needs, etc. Duplicate dibs are OK—but try to balance your reports so that as many different pages of the text as possible are covered. Specify the page numbers you are covering, both in placing dibs and also in making your application reports. In writing a report, focus on your application of the Hitch & Youatt concept. (Successes, failures, theory that doesn’t translate to real life—all are appropriate to report.) Postings to the Hitch & Youatt discussion board may be made at any time up to April 22, but with no more than 2 reports made in a single week. 5 reports at 3 points each = 15 points.

8.      Participation in online workshops.  Some Track 2 students will require “attendance” of their workshops by members of this class. When the number of workshops is known, assignments for everyone to serve as “Online Students” will be made. Performance as a student will not be graded, only participation. Total across the semester: 10 points.

9.      If substituting this course for EDUC 5043, 15 hours of field experience in a family and consumer sciences classroom is required. In order to observe the required number of hours, you may use up to eight (8) hours of the required time to reflect on your own classroom teaching. If you are the only family and consumer sciences teacher in your school, ask your principal to suggest a master teacher in another teaching area who you can observe during your conference period. An observation log and reflective journal must be kept. The observation log should note the date and time of each observation and the teacher observed (include the same information for each of your own classes that you use for observation). Following each classroom visit, reflect on what you observed: what teaching or management strategies did you see? What specific learner characteristics did you observe? How did the environment enhance/deter learning? What did you learn from your visit that you will use or not use in the classroom? Failure to complete the field experience will result in failure of the class. Submit two copies of the log/journal.

Family Sciences Diversity Policy:

 

The concept of diversity implies a total commitment to an open context that promotes equality, social tolerance, and the right to express different points of view. The Family Sciences Department practices and celebrates diversity and creates a culture characterized by a climate of inclusion rather than exclusion, where people are involved in working together for a common good. Diversity refers to the inclusion of all persons regardless of their differences. These differences include but are not limited to ethnicity, race, culture, gender, age, socio-economic status, religion, sexual orientation, and physical disability. The Family Sciences Department does not condone discrimination in any form and complies with Texas Woman’s University Non-Discrimination Policy. The faculty actively seek enrichment through the strength, power, and wisdom of diversity.
