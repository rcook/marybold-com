Catalog Description:

FS 5123. Theories of the Family. Theoretical approaches to the family; the nature and functions of the family; its relationship to institutions, culture, and the individual. Three lecture hours a week. Credit: Three hours.

 

 

Course Objectives:

  1. Develop an understanding of the theoretical bases for family science.
  2. Apply major theoretical frameworks to specific research areas in the family field.
  3. Be responsive to the contextual issues that shape the origins and development of major theoretical frameworks.
  4. Critically evaluate family theories.

 

 

Course Requirements:

  1. Class attendance and participation. (Absences exceeding four (4) hours makes an A not possible.) Attend all classes and participate in discussions and activities. Attendance and participation at on-campus meetings = 10% of semester grade.
  2. Contribute to a Theory Timeline, created as a threaded discussion in Blackboard. Use the Family Studies Timeline on the 5123 CD-ROM v 1.0 as a starting point. Submit a total of five (5) timeline entries, for a variety of eras. Each entry should run 2 or 3 sentences, with enough detail to be meaningful. If your source is the Boss text, include a reference with page number like this: (Boss-200). Other sources should be written in APA format. At the end of each entry, include your initials for credit. Last date for submissions: February 28. Timeline submissions = 10% of semester grade.
  3. Actively participate on a theory team, communicating either through Blackboard or private e-mail. Keep Bold posted on progress of your team’s work. Family Therapy and Counseling majors may NOT sign up for Systems theory; Child Development majors may NOT sign up Family Development Theory or Life Course Theory; Family Studies majors may NOT sign up for Ecological Theory.
  4. As a theory team, host a Blackboard discussion for the week assigned to your theory. Utilize the documents on the 5123 CD-ROM v 1.0 as starting points for the discussion. You can assume that classmates have read about the theory with these documents in mind. As hosts, you can decide how you want class members to comment: perhaps by answering study questions you pose at the start of the week, perhaps by contributing to a daily log in which you stimulate comments on a specific issue, perhaps by participating in an online debate or skit playing assigned parts, perhaps by assigning a vocabulary quiz or activity, perhaps by writing a scavenger hunt for information. Do not be afraid to experiment with new methods. You may direct class members concerning number of comments, length of comments, dates for comments, etc. You will find the Boss text rich in information to discuss; consider including the pertinent Application in the Boss text as this section frequently points to alternative views about the theory. Theory host activity = 10% of semester grade.
  5. As a class member, actively participate in each week of discussion. (Note: your access to Blackboard is through an Internet browser. Thus, you can make your contributions from home, from a hotel, from an airport….) Participation = 10% of semester grade.
  6. Contribute to a web sitelet called Theory Sampler. Topics will be assigned in class. Study the graduate student site at http://twu.edu/~f_bold/divorce/divorce as example. Submissions for the Theory Sampler will be strictly limited to 400 - 500 words (not including references). Research and writing should be scrupulously clean. Cite at least three (3) references, and use the Boss text no more than once. You will be on a writing team that focuses on a particular theory; this theory will NOT match your theory team focus. (Note that each person on the team will write an essay up to 500 words.) As a writing team you will be responsible for editing each others’ reference lists as well as rough drafts. The team will share a grade on the editing of the reference lists. Submissions to Bold should be in html (using a mock page that can be downloaded from http://twu.edu/~f_bold/theory/sampler/mock.htm) or in plain text with no formatting. Last date for submissions: March 15. Earlier submissions will be appreciated. Essay submission = = 10% of semester grade. Score on edited reference lists = 10% of semester grade.
  7. Access documents on electronic reserve for this class and select a number of articles to write on. (Document list will also be posted in Blackboard; some articles will be accessible through Blagg-Huey online databases.) If your desired grade in this course is a B, you may select as few as two (2) articles, with a paper word count between 250 and 300 words (not counting references). For a B, your due date is March 31. If your desired grade in this course is an A, you must select at least ten (10) articles. You will decide if your synthesis of ideas belongs in one or several papers. You are welcome to add more source material, as long as you meet the minimum number from this reading list. Your total word count (whether in one paper or several papers combined) must be between 1000 and 1250 words (not counting references). For an A, your due date is April 15. All papers must be submitted electronically, with strong preference for Microsoft Word. In grading, Bold will check two things first: word count and reference list. If the word count is too long or the reference list has more than 3 errors, the paper will be returned un-graded. Paper(s) = 10% of semester grade.
  8. Mid-term exam and final exam. To earn a B in this course, each exam grade must be B- or better. To earn an A in this course, each exam grade must be B+ or better. The mid-term exam will consist of objective items. The final exam will be several essay questions in a mock comps setting. Mid-term exam is 10% of semester grade. Final exam is 20% of semester grade.

 

Policies and Procedures:

  1. Written work should be typed. The style manual for the American Psychological Association (APA), 5th ed., should be used for citations, references, and manuscript style.
  2. Written work should be turned in according to class schedule. Grade deductions will be made for late work.
  3. I welcome e-mail submissions. Attachments should be in Microsoft Word. (Website submissions will follow different specs.) I acknowledge every assignment with a message that “XXX was received in good order.” If you submit an assignment and do not receive this reply within a few days, you should assume that I did not receive your assignment. Due dates are firm regardless of server problems.

 

Evaluation Criteria:

 

A: 90 – 100%            B: 80 – 89    C: 70 – 79    D: 60 – 69    F: 59 down

B+ work means a score of 87 to 89, inclusive.

B- work means a score of 80 to 82, inclusive.

 

Assignments are weighted as described above under Course Requirements; however, note that some assignments require a minimum grade achieved in order to qualify for a certain semester grade

 

Written work, whether in Blackboard or a paper or a test, must display an understanding of the topic or question. Your responses must “fit” the question. If you invent a new question or take your answer down an alternative path, I must assume that you do not know the answer to the question posed. For grading purposes, this would equate to turning in a blank sheet.

 

Participation in class and in Blackboard discussion is encouraged and expected. Domination of discussion is not. As a graduate student you should be quite capable of self-monitoring your contribution to group learning. You are in a room (or online community) of smart people, most of whom are leaders in some arena. In graduate school, you may occasionally find yourself in a learning group that will benefit from your leadership skills or specialty knowledge; but most of the time you will not. Most of the time, the group will benefit most from your carefully considered and concise contribution to the topic and from your insight into the dynamics of the group.

 

Incompletes should be rarely needed in graduate school. I expect graduate students to complete the requirements of a course in the semester specified or drop the course and re-take it later. (After all, everyone would benefit from tacking on winter break or summer vacation to finish a paper….)
