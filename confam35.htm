Grandparent-headed home may refer to grandparents rearing a child in the parents’ absence, or it may refer to an extended family household with all three generations present. Without an operational definition, statistics may be misleading. Nevertheless, most demographers agree that there is a definite trend in more grandparents supporting their grandchildren financially and also in surrogate parenting. This discussion focuses on grandparents who are serving as surrogate parents because their own son or daughter is not available. This represents about 1/3 of the cases of children living in the grandparent home. (So, in about 2/3 of cases, a parent is also in the grandparent home with the child.)

The U.S. Census Bureau profiles typical caregiving grandparents as about 60 years old. They believe that the caregiving arrangement will be permanent. They are more likely to be African-American or Hispanic-American. Caucasian children in similar need are more likely to be placed in foster care.

Proportion of children living in grandparent homes:
12% of African-American children

6% of Hispanic-American children

4% of Caucasian children

(Mullen, 1996)

Common reasons for grandparents becoming children’s caregivers (Pinson-Millburn, Fabian, Schlossberg, & Pyle, 1996):

  • Increase in drug abuse, especially crack cocaine (the major cause)

  • Increase in out-of-wedlock births

  • Child abuse or neglect

  • Incarceration of parent (frequently for drug-related crimes)

  • Death of the parent

  • Unemployment of the parent

  • Health issues, including HIV/AIDS

  • Divorce

When grandparents assume parenting roles for young children, they face numerous problems. In summary, they include:

Financial issues — When government assistance is sought, the monthly help may be about $100/month. This compares to foster are payments of $400/month and higher. Grandparents are not eligible to receive foster care payments.
Insurance issues— Their regular health insurance may not extend to grandchildren, especially if grandparents do not pursue formal adoption. Even legal guardianship does not guarantee coverage as a dependent.
Guardianship issues — Without formal paperwork establishing grandparents’ status, problems can arise in many settings, from seeking medical treatment (even a well-child check-up) to signing permission slips for school field trips. Some reasons guardianship is not pursued:
  • Grandparent may feel reluctant to admit the caregiving arrangement is permanent.
  • Parent may refuse to relinquish rights.
  • Grandparent may not be able to predict the parent’s willingness to cooperate.
  • Grandparent may be unaware of legal processes.
Educational issues — Grandparent may feel awkward in dealing with the child’s school. When a problem is presented, grandparent may feel overwhelmed by institutional requirements.
Social issues — Grandparent may feel inadequate to supervise a child’s social concerns and “modern” problems.
Discipline issues — Grandparent may feel a sense of failure if the child’s parent is absent due to drug use or a similar problem. The older person may ask “what if I make the same mistake with this child?” or may take a passive role in setting limits for the child.

Good reports on grandparents as caregivers (Pinson-Millburn, Fabian, Schlossberg, & Pyle, 1996):

  • Some research shows that the children score well on health, academic, and behavior measures. Many score better than children living in single-parent homes; some score better than children living in two-parent homes.

  • Although some children must cope with developmental and emotional problems, they may well have the help and support of grandparents who are eager to assume the task of rearing them.
