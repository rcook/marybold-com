Resiliency, strength

As a research topic, resiliency is very new: early pioneers such as Emmy Werner of UC-Davis have been researching and writing since the 1960s. But the big surge of interest dates just to the 1980s. Since that time, resiliency has been studied both on the individual level and the family level.

Much of the research has centered on these findings. Some came as surprises to the researchers.. 

  • Environments—even wretched ones—don’t necessarily dictate a person’s outcome.

  • As many as 10% of young children seem to be extraordinarily hardy and resistant to damage.

  • Even in abusive settings, about 33% of children are taking good care of themselves by the time they reach adolescence or young adulthood.

  • Different studies report percentages between 10% and 33% of children who are resilient even though their parents are neglectful of family needs (because of substance abuse, for example). 

  • In adulthood, more than 80% of people who were abused say that they have built good lives for themselves.

  • On at least some level, resiliency can be learned.

  • Family resiliency can be enhanced by improved communication (Walsh, 1998).

  • Family strength has identifiable components such as communication, shared values, commitment, time spent together, appreciation, coping ability (Stinnett & DeFrain, 1985).

These are some of the leaders in resiliency research:

Nick Stinnett and John DeFrain — 1985 University of Nebraska-Lincoln, study of 3500 strong families
Norman Garmezy — University of Minnesota, late 1980s study of children of severely depressed mothers
Michael Rutter — UK study of children of mothers suffering drug addiction
Emmy Werner — UC-Davis psychologist who began longitudinal study in the 1960s of Hawaiians who live in a severely economically depressed community
Edith Grotberg — international resilience project administered from the University of Alabama-Birmingham
Sybil and Steve Wolin — psychologists who teach Survivor’s Pride
David Miller — Case Western Reserve University social work professor studying resiliency of at-risk, inner-city children
Peggye Dilworth-Anderson — University of North Carolina-Greensboro scholar studying resilient African-American families
Ernestine Brown — University of Alabama psychologist
Froma Walsh — 1998 book, Strengthening Family Resilience
