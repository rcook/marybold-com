Formal Rules

Privacy—Protect your own and honor others’. Don’t share your password with anyone, and don’t snoop for other people’s.

Flaming—Definition: blasting or attacking someone. Most organizations consider flaming an abuse of the system. If you are flamed, try not to respond. If you feel like flaming someone, count to 10 and remind yourself that angry e-mail burns bridges.

Spamming—Definition: sending unsolicited advertising to a computer or a FAX machine. Junk e-mail is illegal in the U.S.

Copyright—Do not assume that everything on the Internet is public domain. The vast majority of material is copyright-protected as soon as it is created, and does not require © registration, or even © notice. Copyright lasts the lifetime of the creator, plus 50 years. Protect your own work with this notice format: © 2000 by Jane Doe.

 

Informal Rules

Tone of voice—Adopt a friendly tone of voice. This can be as simple as starting the message with Dear Margaret, and signing off with Regards, Mary. Do not flame [see definition under Formal Rules] and do not respond to flaming.

Use of humor—Beware…without voice inflection and facial expression, your humor may be misinterpreted. A friendly “Duh” in person comes across as snide in e-mail. Recommended: use humor sparingly or only with clues. A clue in informal communication may be a parenthetical stage direction after your joke [(Grin), (Ha-ha), (Joke), etc.], explaining your meaning.

Use of emoticons—Even people who never draw a smiley face with a pen will find occasional good use of these characters. “Emoticon” is a combination of “emotion” and “icon.” Recommended: stick to the well-known basic ones. [See chart under Emoticons]

Clear Communications—

  • 1) Be brief. 
  • 2) If including questions or points to consider, list them in separate paragraphs or number them. 
  • 3) Specify what action, date, etc., that you require of your reader.

Readability—Build in white space with paragraph breaks and indents. But don’t try to “design” the page, since its format will likely change when downloaded to your reader’s computer.

ALL CAPS IN MESSAGES—ALL CAPS mean I AM SHOUTING MAD! Since they are also the hardest style of type for the human eye to read, you have two reasons to avoid them.

Introducing yourself and saying goodbye—In submitting class assignments via e-mail, follow your professor’s request for identification. For example, I always ask students to begin the communication with a line like this: This Jane Doe, answering questions #2, #5, and #10 from the Bigge & Shermis reading. (On the other hand, some professors ask that student names appear only in the From: line of the message, in order to evaluate without focusing on who the writer is.)

In the workplace, a brief reminder in a very short first paragraph is appropriate: We met on March 10 and agreed that my report could be submitted via e-mail. Attached you will find.…

In closing, Sincerely and Regards are always safe to use. Informal closings (See you later, etc. ) should be reserved for correspondence with friends.
