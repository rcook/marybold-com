Listserv v. Group E-mail

Size of group is the usual determinant for creating a listserv for group communication rather than relying on “group e-mail.” Privacy and security may also be issues, however. While all Internet-based communications are potentially public and open to scrutiny, the perception of privacy may dictate choices. Whose perception? Ideally, the participants in the communication medium would have the opportunity to vote on which route to follow. In some settings, an institution’s server would be assumed to be the more protective communication medium. However, many people (especially those who meet face-to-face in addition to using computer mediated communication) may feel increased locus of control by creating their own mailing list for the group of participants.

Arguments in favor of creating a listserv:

  • Set-up is handled by someone else, as is maintenance if participants drop-out or change e-mail addresses. All members are enrolled at the same time; additional members can be added later.
  • Each person’s e-mail address is known to the listserv manager but will not be known to other participants unless a message is posted to the listserv. This provides relative anonymity for the participant who chooses to “lurk.”
  • Each person can enter the listserv by simply typing in the listserv address in a mail message or clicking on the saved listserv address in an e-mail address book. By contrast, group e-mail requires entering multiple addresses in an address book and storing them on the computer. In case of hardware or software failure, the e-mail addresses may be erased from participants’ personal computers..
  • A listserv manager can provide a certain amount of supervision of messages posted to the listserv. When inappropriate messages are posted, an “institutional” reprimand may be accomplished. In group e-mail, participants may feel awkward in dealing with inappropriate use of the communication medium and may spend time and energy managing reprimands, apologies, face-saving interactions, and so forth.
  • Some automatic safeguards can be built into the listserv program so that irritants such as duplicate postings can be minimized. Similarly, listserv participants can be protected from excessively long messages that may tie up systems and clog mailboxes.

Arguments in favor of using group e-mail:

  • The group is not reliant on an institution’s server that may be “down” on occasion.
  • Set-up can be accomplished immediately without being scheduled with a server manager (although set-up does depend on coöperation of all group members).
  • Self-management provides locus of control, which may be important to some individuals. If the number of participants is small, creating or maintaining mail lists is not a burden.
  • The group is not supervised by server managers or any one else who may have access to listservs operating on the server.
  • Communication can continue indefinitely, whereas some servers may impose limits by semester, for example.
  • Novice e-mail users may prefer a personal approach to computer-mediated group communication.

Working with Tech Support

Consult with a technical expert (hereafter called “Tech Support”) from the start of planning a listserv, even if initial contact is only to ascertain availability of resources/services.

After conducting a needs assessment of your group, share plans, priorities, and preferences with Tech Support. A major decision will be whether the listserv will be moderated or unmoderated.  Work with both the group and Tech Support in order to disseminate how-to instructions, publicity about the listserv (if appropriate), and listserv netiquette.

Consult both your group and Tech Support about concerns—AKA horror stories. This short list alludes to the magnitude of headaches that can accompany listserv and group e-mail communication:

  • Angry at not being able to “unsubscribe” from a listserv, a user attached a large executable file to a “flaming” message and sent it on automatic pilot to the listserv on a Friday evening. By Monday morning, every user had received 250+ copies of the message and attachment.
  • Irritated by a “newbie’s” elementary questions to the listserv, a user SHOUTED at the entire list and made rude comments that may very well have made someone cry.
  • Eager to be responsive to other people’s comments, a user replied with comments such as “Neat idea! Way to go!” and distributed the message to the entire list…frequently.
  • Intent on straightening out an upcoming schedule, a user responded to Ms. X’s posting with “Glad I saw your name today—I’ve been meaning to tell you that we need to change our lunch date from 12:00 to 1:00,” sending the message to the entire list instead of to only Ms. X.

Listserv Netiquette

These are typical rules for listservs. This list is not complete! (This material is a mix of material drawn from many online sources.)

  • Persons posting messages not related to the interests of the group will be warned once that continued posting of unrelated items to this list will result in subsequent removal from the list without warning.
  • Persons engaging in “flaming,” advertising, or sending profane, lewd, illicit, or illegal messages to the list will be summarily removed from the list without warning.
  • If you reply to a message you receive from the list, the list is set to automatically reply to the sender only, not to the entire list. If you wish the entire list to see your reply, please e-mail a copy of your reply to the regular posting address. (Note: a listserv may alternately automatically reply to the full list, requiring users to enter a different e-mail address when intending to send to a single person.)
  • A listserv is like an electronic bulletin board—it allows subscribers to write and respond to messages sent to an entire group of people. When you e-mail to the listserv, all subscribers read your message. When you submit a message, it should be something that you want to share with the entire mailing list, since your submission is distributed to all of the other people on that list. 
  • Sending a message in all caps (UPPER CASE) implies that the sender is shouting. This is offensive to some recipients.
  • Messages sent to a Listserv should pertain to the particular Listserv. Users should not send or respond to personal messages, or messages that don't pertain to the Listserv. Many people consider receiving a message that doesn’t apply to them “junk” email. Most users find it annoying.
  • A user may inadvertently send an inappropriate message. When this happens, Listmembers should not exacerbate the problem by replying to the list with a public reprimand to the person who posted in error. Listmembers who want to help new users to use the list properly should address messages privately to the individual who misposted in the first place.
