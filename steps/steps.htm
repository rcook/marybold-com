Summary

An interactive study unit titled “Roles of Grandparents: Playmate, Mentor, Caregiver” was created for use with 26 undergraduate students in a Social Gerontology course. The unit, an HTML (Hypertext Markup Language) document, was introduced to students as a required component of the course. It was available for access through the Department of Family Sciences home page on the Internet.

The authors designed the project with three student-oriented goals. The first goal was to increase student familiarity with the Internet and with computer mediated communication (e-mail), thereby promoting critical thinking and reflective communication. The second goal was to encourage students’ use of materials outside of class to prepare for in-class discussion, thereby using class time for the processing, rather than the delivery, of information. The third goal was to support students’ use of the technology to access Internet sites pertinent to Family Sciences, thereby stimulating students to make use of the Internet for their studies.

Students were required to submit two e-mail messages answering questions posed in “Roles of Grandparents.” Students were also required to access other Internet sites that were relevant to the course. After all students had completed the assignments, the instructor distributed a course evaluation which included survey questions about students’ experiences with and opinions of the computer component.

“Roles of Grandparents” was written as a hypertext document for delivery over the Internet. This topic was chosen because of the emphasis on the new role of grandparent currently in Family Sciences curricula. “Roles of Grandparents” presented recent research about grandparents alongside references to the classic literature frequently cited in undergraduate textbooks.

The authors used the 10-step procedure outlined below in their work. Steps 1 through 3 constitute the planning stage of the project. Steps 4 through 8 are concerned with the design and construction of the hypertext document. Steps 9 and 10 comprise the publication stage.

Step 1: Determination of purpose, topic, and depth

Purpose: The purpose of a hypertext document should be stated much as the purpose of any published document is stated. While the medium of the Internet demands a different style of publishing than print publishing, the statement of purpose for both should be concise and focused. The nature of electronic publishing allows the following special features to be included in a publishing purpose: asynchronous communication, interactivity, electronic storage, choice of format (for example, on-screen or print-out), and so forth.

The purpose of “Roles of Grandparents” was to present curriculum material for access by students over the Internet. As a first effort by the authors, “Roles of Grandparents” was viewed as an experimental teaching tool and therefore an additional purpose was to assess efficacy of and student reaction to such a presentation.

The choice of topic for a hypertext document may be considered from the aspect of utilizing the technology to best advantage. For example, the document can include recent developments or recent research, since the time from event to publication can be short. Information can be revised frequently, also. Some topics are enhanced by graphic images. The cost of images (in color, 3D, and motion) in electronic documents is a fraction of the cost in print documents. Some topics are similarly enhanced by interactivity with the student. Hypertext, even at its most elementary use, involves the student by giving choices with links to more material.

Grandparenting was selected as the topic for this project because of curriculum emphasis on new roles of grandparents in family and society. The topic was addressed in limited space in the textbook for the course. Recent sources could be included in this hypertext document. Although heavily text-based, the document was organized in quiz format which involved the student in making selections and “jumping” to new screens of information.

Hypertext materials can be designed to be “broad” or “deep.” A broad document provides hyperlinks to many topics or to many web sites. Such links aid in broadening the scope of the study. A deep document links to more detailed information about the topics, perhaps to definitions or examples of specific terms or to complete documentation of an idea. The linking power of HTML supports both goals equally well.

For this project, the authors focused on only three roles of grandparents and limited the amount of information conveyed. The authors chose this path in order to avoid students’ feeling overwhelmed by a large amount of reading. This decision was based on an assumption that for the large majority of students, “Roles of Grandparents” would constitute their first curriculum reading in this medium. In order to serve the more experienced computer users among the students, challenge was provided in the large number of study questions provided for student choice in the assignment. Half of the study questions could be answered briefly and easily; the other 15 questions invited critical thinking and more complex answers. (A small number of students did select some of these more difficult questions.)

Step 2: Compliance with regulations and guidelines

Inherent in the electronic publishing process is the necessity of recognition of publishing requirements, especially when university server space is to be utilized. Requirements and guidelines affecting this work included the University’s rules, copyright law, and other universities&#c resources.

Rules: As a relative newcomer to Internet publishing—a status shared by the majority of U.S. universities—the University’s technical services currently center primarily on hardware and access issues. At the time of the authors’ work, rules regarding publication were limited to specifying who may authorize the creation of a web page and the requisite name, e-mail, and date to appear on every home page.

Copyright law: The law and ethics of copyrighting constitute a challenge in the digital environment. Knowledge and understanding of copyright law has never been widespread. In an environment that permits (some would say, promotes) copyright infringement with ease, as is the case in the digital environment, that knowledge and understanding is virtually nonexistent. As a result, copyright infringement has become routine by some computer users. Lawmakers suggest that copyright laws in the U.S. should be revised in light of the evolving computing environment. In the meantime, professionals using computers and the Internet should adhere to the restrictions of copyright law and set an example for students to do the same.

The authors of “Roles of Grandparents” strived to follow all standards regarding the use of copyrighted material. Copyright law was a factor in the selection of three graphics used in the hypertext document. One graphic, a sandwich representing multiple generations and the “sandwich generation,” was selected from a collection of clip art available on CD-ROM. As is typical of most clip art sold for computer use, the image can be used in virtually any fashion other than re-sale as an illustration.

Another graphic, a head-and-shoulders representation of an older male, was scanned from a pen-and-ink drawing. The drawing, by artist Carol Small of Arkansas, was purchased as a work for hire for an earlier print publishing project by one of the “Roles of Grandparents” authors. Such re-use of an illustration is appropriate when the copyright holder grants permission for the use. In this case, the copyright holder was not the artist since the drawing was sold by the artist as a work for hire. In such a situation, the purchaser becomes the copyright holder and determines all future uses of the work. The copyright holder granted permission for use of the image in all print and electronic versions of “Roles of Grandparents.”

The last graphic, a vine on a pole representing “beanpole family composition,” was scanned from a pen-and-ink drawing by one of the authors. The copyright holder granted permission for use of the image in all print and electronic versions of “Roles of Grandparents.”

Other universities’ resources: The educational community is historically a sharing community. In the electronic age, educators continue in this tradition. Consequently, there is a great deal of information about instructional technology available through web sites.

The authors located several electronic publishing guides at the web sites of other universities. Most helpful was material produced by TeamWeb at the University of Texas at Austin. These guidelines address general publishing concerns, copyright, and electronic mail, as well as technical concerns such as web page design and use of HTML.

Step 3: Determination of available resources

Resources for HTML authoring (and electronic publishing, in general) are increasing in number and in quality. How-to information has become commonly available in a very short period of time. Scholarly research on uses of HTML documents and related educational tools remains limited, especially in comparison with the extensive use of such tools. Local resources are sometimes available not only in universities’ technical services but in other departments and colleges.

Reviewing others’ work on the WWW, including syllabi and course materials, was especially helpful. The authors also learned from advice and example by professors in three other departments at and by speakers at a national conference.

For example, practical tips were garnered at the NCFR Workshop on Instructional Technology at the 1996 Annual Conference in Kansas City. Dean Busby of Syracuse University recommended that educators bypass their campus servers and use the Internet. David Klein of the University of Notre Dame stated that a class size of about 25 is the (practical) maximum number of students in a course which includes e-mail communication. Randal Day of Washington State University offered his interpretation of the impact of visual images for the MTV generation. This type of information, reflecting educators’ first-hand experience, influenced many of the authors’ decisions in this project.

Step 4: Software and hardware considerations

HTML code—the basis for hypertext documents to be viewed on the WWW—can be written in any word processing program as a text file. Authoring in this manner requires learning HTML, one of easier, higher-level computer languages.

Most educators prefer to use a software program called an HTML “editor” rather than learn HTML. An editor allows the user to ignore much of the actual code, since the software automatically writes it “behind the scenes,” as the user enters text on a screen similar to a word processing screen.

An Internet browser such as Netscape or Explorer is used to preview the HTML document. Thus, the user can see exactly how the document will appear on the Internet before it is uploaded to the WWW.

Virtually any Windows 95 computer has sufficient hardware to run this type of software. Computers with less than 16 megabytes of RAM (Random Access Memory) may have fewer software choices among full HTML editors.

For “Roles of Grandparents,” the authors worked with two editors. The first draft was prepared with Macromedia Backstage Designer Plus. Final work was done with Microsoft FrontPage. Both editors are full feature programs; FrontPage includes more web management tools. The authors found FrontPage a more intuitive program and more user-friendly.

Step 5: Structural layout of the home page

Hypertext permits flexibility in document layout and page design. Parenthetical information and more detailed information can be organized in pages off the main document. For the student, the obvious benefit is selection of detail as needed.

For the author or teacher, the less obvious benefit is a novel approach to organizing material. While the process is time-consuming, the resulting document is strengthened by features such as indexed content, cross-referencing, quick access to a glossary, and access to more detail (for example: original text, raw data, hypertext links to sources). Utilizing computer technology and HTML, authors can create lengthy and complex documents at virtually no publication cost other than labor.

In “Roles of Grandparents,” the authors utilized hypertext layout for interest hooks in the form of quiz questions. In answering the quiz, students followed links to research-based curriculum material. (Students’ answers to the quiz were not recorded and students were advised of this.)

Step 6: Display options and preparation of text and images

At present, most WWW access involves the use of graphical browsers produced by Netscape and Microsoft Corporation. Text browsers such as Lynx are also in use, but by fewer people.

The current standard for display on the WWW and graphical browsers is home pages created in HTML. Graphic images can be incorporated easily in HTML as GIF files.

The majority of campus computers for student use provide Internet access with the browser Netscape Navigator. A smaller number of computers on campus display Microsoft Explorer. Although all students have free Internet access with Lynx, the authors decided to proceed as though Lynx would be used rarely or never. This was based on informal conversations with students in the Department.

Components of “Roles of Grandparents” were prepared as either HTML or GIF files. A total of 27 HTML files and 3 GIF files were created.

Step 7: Construction of the main menu

As in any publication, the electronic document should open with a clear and interest-provoking introduction. In print, publishers and authors rely on elements such as abstracts, introductions, book covers, and graphics. On the WWW, publishers and authors can use any of the same elements—or all of them. For example, the opening screen may resemble a book cover with graphics. It may include links to other screens where the abstract or the introduction may be found. Other links might allow users to go directly to a references section or to related sites by the same authors or by others in the field.

The main menu of “Roles of Grandparents” was designed so that students would immediately recognize it as the correct “place” to find their assignment. Graphic images were arranged for quick loading and also so that they would be visible in a partial or full view on even small monitors. Thus, the user would immediately see the options represented by the three images. (This assumes that the type sizes on the user’s computer would be similar to those specified in the HTML code. The issue is discussed further in Step 8.)

On-screen instruction was kept to a minimum, allowing users to move into the quiz directly. To support first-time users, the authors prepared a printed page of information which the instructor distributed to students.

Step 8: Inputting text and graphics

HTML has a limited range of typesetting options. For example, only six levels—or sizes—of headings exist. Although more variety in type size can be achieved with graphics, such strategies are not intuitive for the beginning or occasional HTML author.

Similar constraints exist for body type. HTML provides a “normal” size and several smaller sizes typically reserved for footnotes and the like. Some usual typesetting options, such as italic and underlining, are of limited value since they do not always present clear, readable text on all monitors. The boldface option is safer to use.

Any available typeface on a computer system can be incorporated in HTML; however, if a receiving system does not have the same typeface, it will substitute with a default face. Thus, a carefully typeset document using Optima and Bookman faces might be downloaded on a user’s system with Helvetica and Courier New. Similarly, background colors and colors of links (active and visited) can be controlled by the user through preferences selected to act as defaults overriding HTML code. Default type sizes can also be selected: vision-impaired users may direct their computers to load all web sites with 14- or 18-point type, for example.

End users’ view of the electronic document may differ dramatically from the document as it was uploaded to the Internet. This fact should influence HTML authors in design decisions in at least some matters. For example, knowing that the majority of end users are unlikely to have Palton Light loaded as a typeface, use of the face would simply bring up a font substitution. Better to design the page with the more standard serif face, Times Roman. The vast majority of users will have Times, or Times Roman, or Times New Roman on system and therefore will see the screen as designed (or with only tiny differences in size or spacing).

Another source of design concern is not knowing what version of browser the end user may have. For example, on campus, computers display a range of Netscape Navigator, from version 2.0 to 4.1. Certain screen and typeface views differ according to the version of browser. The arrangement of text in a table created in version 3.0 may not be supported under version 2.0, for example. While the text may appear on screen, it may not be in the intended format. Similarly, if an early version of browser does not support frames, entire web sites may not be accessible.

In designing “Roles of Grandparents,“ the authors chose to use Times Roman as the typeface. Not only is the typeface a common one on computer systems, but it is also the most “readable” of proportional faces in the U.S. Readability refers to the ability of the human eye to scan and interpret characters. Times Roman is, for practical purposes, transparent to the American reader: it does not attract attention. Only standard HTML type sizes were utilized. These choices, along with the decision to forego a frames layout, accommodated what the authors considered likely browser versions ranging from Netscape Navigator 2.0 to 4.0.

Graphic images are incorporated in HTML documents using two file types: GIF and JPG. Usual pronunciations are jiff (less frequently, giff) and jay-peg. Graphics are easily identified with the appropriate file extensions (image.gif or image.jpg). Well-suited to photographs, the JPG format provides rich shading. (In print, the equivalent would be graphics produced in process color, or four-color CYMK.) Although image data is compressed in all file formats, JPG files are relatively large and slow-loading. The GIF format, by contrast, produces an image in a relatively small memory space and is consequently quick-loading. Line drawings are good candidates for GIF files, as are colored images that can be reproduced with a limited number of colors. (In print, the equivalent would be graphics produced with spot color.) With some degradation expected, photographs can be scanned, converted to GIF format, and used on the Internet.

All images in “Roles of Grandparents” were well suited to the GIF format. Produced as small graphics for the main menu, the three load relatively quickly. On subsequent pages, the same images appear in larger size and take longer to load.

Two of the images (man and beanpole) were pen-and-ink line drawings owned by one of the authors. They were scanned and converted to digital format, and saved as GIF files. The third (sandwich) was computer clip-art. For this purpose, it was converted to GIF format.

Step 9: Uploading the program to an Internet site

Files can be uploaded to the Internet using FTP (File Transfer Protocol) software. HTML and graphic files can be uploaded together. Gaining access to a server is the first step. In most university settings, uploading to official homepages is a task reserved for specific individuals with coded access.

At the University, faculty, staff, and students enjoy space on the University server for individual homepages. Uploading can therefore be accomplished by any individual with a University e-mail account. However, since “Roles of Grandparents” was to serve as curriculum material for students, the authors opted to submit the program for inclusion in the homepage of the Department of Family Sciences. There, it is part of a subsection called Electronic Publications of the Department.

As a Department publication, “Roles of Grandparents” could be uploaded to the Internet only by the Department secretary. The task involved uploading 27 HTML files and 3 GIF files. Total time was about five minutes; the actual transfer of a single file was typically measured in seconds since “Roles of Grandparents” was not a large-memory program.

Step 10: Periodic review of the home page

Electronic documents should be reviewed by authors to insure that hyperlinks continue to operate. If links are provided to outside URLs, the need for review becomes critical. The nature of the Internet is one of change, which means that related sites (and additional sites related to those sites) may be altered at any time. With regular review, authors can determine if their own document needs revision to reflect changes in linked URLs.

“Roles of Grandparents” uses only relative URLs in its links. This means that all the links are to areas within the Department of Family Sciences home page. Consequently, the need for on-line review is not great. The authors did, however, test all links and view all screens in order to confirm that what students would find did indeed match the program submitted for uploading to the Internet.

Student Assignment

Students were instructed to access “Roles of Grandparents” on the Internet. Class time was scheduled in a nearby computer lab and students were instructed on how to enter the URL on Internet browsers. The instructor accompanied the students to the lab and assisted students when asked. A small number of students elected to access the program from home or the University library. Written instructions were provided.

“Roles of Grandparents” included 30 study questions regarding the content. Students were told to select any two questions and submit answers to the instructor via e-mail. The answers were to include an introductory sentence such as, “This is Jane Doe answering question #1” and to be limited to no more than 250 words each. The instructor announced that answers could be statements of opinion, although students were also encouraged to incorporate information from textbook readings, lecture notes, and class discussion. Students were advised that the majority of scoring points would be awarded on the basis of two answers being transmitted electronically. Further, writing mechanics such as spelling and format would not be evaluated. A due date was set for 3 weeks, with the assurance that students needing personal help at that point would not be penalized for late submissions.

The second requirement was to access several web sites pertinent to the subject of social gerontology. The instructor provided a list of examples but also encouraged students to explore the Internet on their own, using search engines to locate other URLs. Students were not required to document their results, although about half the students did print out information from web sites as reference material for another assignment for the course.

Results of the E-mail Component

Twenty-six students submitted answers to two questions of their choice. The numbered questions were as follows:

1. List personal behaviors or shared activities which might identify a grandparent as a playmate to grandchildren.

2. Many researchers comment on the diversity of family forms and styles of grandparenting. How might such diversity relate to Emick and Hayslip’s suggestion that established grandparenting roles in the research literature are not accurate now?

3. How does the playmate style of grandparenting integrate the norm of noninterference?

4. If you have observed the norm of noninterference within an extended family, describe how it was evidenced. (Spoken, unspoken, circumstance causing it to be invoked or noticed, result, etc.)

5. How do modern transportation and high-tech tools affect family communication?

6. Construct a model of a fictional “scattered” family and predict annual costs of visits and communication.

7. What are possible trends for extended families of the future regarding contact between generations?

8. Some researchers hold the opinion that today’s grandparents’ detachment from younger family is harmful, even though most older people say their detachment is their preference. What is your opinion?

9. What is the impact of increasing life spans on how central grandchildren are in grandparents’ lives?

10. What may be the effect on “grandparent consumerism” as baby boomers enter their 60’s (after the year 2005)? What assumptions does this question make?

11. What historical factors have made parent-child relationships egalitarian?

12. The diversity of American families may or may not support an “egalitarian” view. Comment with examples.

13. How important is family harmony?

14. What are some possible positive and negative family reactions to an elder mentor?

15. If your family has included an elder mentor, comment on his or her personal characteristics which contribute(d) to the role of mentor.

16. Which of these mentoring-style roles do you think predominates in the U.S.? [Listed were mentor, “the national guard,” surrogate parent, historian, role model, wizard, nurturer.]

17. Do these roles indicate one-way or reciprocal relationships?

18. If most households in the past did not include the elderly, how have we come to adopt an image that they did?

19. Do you think grandchildren will be a “rare commodity” in the future?

20. What are some of the factors which affect step-grandparenting? Will those factors be any different in the future?

21. Comment on this statistic: 33% of older people are step-grandparents. (Discuss any aspect of what this statistic means to you. If you do not feel sure about drawing conclusions, compose a list of pertinent questions.)

22. What concerns, questions should we have in reviewing longevity statistics, fertility and mortality rates, family composition statistics, etc.?

23. Why might a caregiving grandparent choose not to secure legal guardianship/adoption of a child, even when the child’s parent is not in the child’s life?

24. Using non-emergency situations, list some of the normative circumstances in an American child’s life which call for informal or formal consent by an adult.

25. What are possible results of caregiving grandparents’ inability to secure preventive health care for children?

26. Should we consider 12% an important figure? [Comparative statistics were listed: 12% of African-American children live with grandparents, 6% of Hispanic-American children live with grandparents, 4% of European-American children live with grandparents.]

27. How can we sort out health, family, and economic causes when reviewing research? What emphasis does ethnicity deserve?

28. Do the above statistics reflect “more problem” for African-Americans, or “more solution” by African-Americans?

29. We have cited researchers’ conclusions about African- and Euro-American households [regarding multigenerational households]. What exceptions should we consider, if any?

30. Can we draw conclusions about “extended family structure” in light of the relatively short history of the U.S. and the influence of immigration during that history?

Timeliness of Submissions

Most students completed their assignments in the allotted time. Two students submitted their e-mail late but did not require additional assistance from the instructor.

Content of Answers

Most of the students’ answers were characterized by personal experience. Virtually every student made a personal or family reference in at least one answer. More than half of the students related a complete “family story“ in at least one answer.

A minority of students included reference to other course materials or class lectures in their answers. Six students’ answers evidenced critical thinking and application of knowledge which may have been gained during this course in social gerontology.

Grading

The computer component comprised less than 10% of the grade for the course. All students received at least 40 points, or 80%, based merely on making two e-mail submissions before the end of the semester. Points above this minimum were based on completeness of thought (within the constraint of 250 words per essay), application of course content, and, to some extent, the student’s willingness to answer difficult questions rather than easy ones. Spelling, grammar, and other mechanics (including format and layout) were not evaluated.

COURSE EVALUATION BY STUDENTS

Instrumentation

A custom instrument was developed for course evaluation of this computer component. The instrument was designed to reflect requirements of the course, using the terminology used in the classroom. Before writing the evaluation survey, the instructor reviewed surveys previously used in research or course evaluation. The survey consisted of questions for demographic data, 30 agree-disagree statements about the computer component, and 10 open-ended questions.

Procedure

The course evaluation for the semester centered on the required computer component. The instructor distributed a print survey to students during a class break on a date following all students’ completion of the assignment. The survey was not a required element of the course, although all students present completed the form.

Answers to open-ended questions

Forty-two percent (n=10) of the students indicated some level of discomfort at first hearing of the Internet component. Comments included “a little fear,” “overwhelmed,” “scared and nervous,” “anxious,” and “panic.” Most of these students also commented that they expected the assignment to be difficult to complete. Most were first-time e-mail users; they were evenly split between traditional and nontraditional.

Almost as many students (n=9) expressed pleasure or excitement at hearing of the assignment. Their comments included “excited,” “happy to have opportunity,” “surprised, happy,” “great,” “curiosity,” and “something different is good.” Most said they expected the assignment to be easy to complete. Only two of these students were first-time e-mail users. Again, they were fairly evenly split between traditional and nontraditional.

Of the remainder, about 21% of the sample (n=5), three were traditional students and two were nontraditional students. Their comments were “It did not phase me,” “Just another busy-work assignment,” “that is would be easy,” “I was not excited about it,” and “It’s about time.”

From the entire sample of 24, 92% said they had adequate time to complete the Internet component; only 2 students said time was not adequate.

Discussion

The successful implementation of the required Internet component of this course allowed realization of three student-oriented goals for the project. The first goal was to increase student familiarity with the Internet and with computer mediated communication, thereby promoting critical thinking and reflective communication. The second goal was to encourage students’ use of materials outside of class to prepare for in-class discussion, thereby using class time for the processing, rather than the delivery, of information. The third goal was to support students’ use of the technology to access Internet sites pertinent to Family Sciences, thereby stimulating students to make use of the Internet for their own inquiry into recent or ongoing work, not just for this course but in their future studies.

An unexpected characteristic of students’ e-mail submissions was the large number of personal stories related. This amount of discussion on personal experiences could not have been accommodated during class time.

Practical considerations for the authors were the time required for the research and writing of the “Roles of Grandparents” document and technical problems in the creation stage. For all of the authors, this represented a first-time effort. A log sheet of work hours would have been helpful in evaluating these stages of work. The authors agree that an estimate of 150 hours is reasonable. They predict much less time commitment would be required for a subsequent project.

For the instructor, practical considerations included timing of the component within the semester, scheduling of labs, and making time available during class and between class to discuss the project with students. Some discussion with students centered on specific questions about the assignment or the delivery of e-mail. However, most out-of-class discussion focused on student’s first-time user status, their need for support, and their private requests to ask “a stupid question” about how e-mail works or how the Internet developed.

The instructor felt that a limit for length (250 words) for each e-mail submission helped control the amount of on-screen reading time (by the instructor) as well as prevented the assignment from seeming overwhelming to students. Only a few students approached the maximum word count. Most wrote between 100 and 150 words.

Errors in spelling, grammar, and writing mechanics were common. Most students clearly were concentrating on the process, not the product. Too, the instructor gave verbal instructions only for inputting messages directly into the e-mail program. No directions were given for importing spell-checked text (in blocks or in files) from a word processor.

The instructor emphasized on several occasions that a goal of the computer component was to introduce new technology in a friendly, stress-free manner. On the day the computer component was first announced, about one-third of the students asked questions and expressed concern about the requirements. One student approached the instructor after class and asked if she should drop the course because of her inexperience with computers.

IMPLICATIONS

Using the Internet for delivery of course materials has numerous implications for students, faculty, and university. Such use eliminates the cost of printing and duplication of assignments. The technology imposes no extra costs to students, assuming they have access to personal, laboratory, or library computers.

The technology allows flexibility in scheduling on-line viewing and allows for options by students in printing out assignments. As students gain experience with such assignments, the flexibility may yield increased efficiency in students’ preparation for class. This flexibility is constrained somewhat if instructors tie the assignment to scheduled computer lab time. However, if course material can be accessed on the World Wide Web, students can review or continue work from home or other computers.

When curriculum web pages include hypertext-based readings, the format engages students whose learning style is well-suited to interactivity. It appears that the technology can broaden educational reach to diverse student populations, especially those who have been unable to travel to campuses. Also, e-mail communications and on-line discussions are formats which stimulate comment from students who may be reluctant to share ideas in class.

Educators in the Family Sciences will need to learn new technical skills as well as devote considerable time to exploring Internet sites in order to remain current on opportunities and resources worldwide, As demand for electronic publication via the Internet increases, these requirements on faculty will also increase.

SUMMARY

“Roles of Grandparents,” an HTML-based document available to undergraduate students through a web site, was created as a required component in a social gerontology course.

The authors determined that 10 steps were necessary in creating a home page for curriculum material. The first 3 steps constituted the planning stage of the project: determine purpose, topic, and depth; comply with regulations and guidelines; determine available resources. Five steps were concerned with the design and construction of the hypertext document: consider software and hardware options; lay-out the home page; consider display options and preparation of text and images; construct the main menu; input text and graphics. The last 2 steps comprised the publication stage: upload the program to an Internet site; review the home page periodically.

Certain practical considerations guided decisions in this project. The HTML document was designed to accommodate viewing with a variety of Internet browsers and with the most common typeface options of personal computers. Assignments were short so as not to overwhelm students new to the technology. Grading standards were liberal and the instructor promoted an atmosphere of anxiety-free learning. The authors estimated that their first effort required about 150 hours of preparation.

The students perceived numerous difficulties arising from required computer components in their coursework. Chief among them were fear, anxiety, and learning to send e-mail. Some students complained about computer lab conditions.

The students also perceived benefits of the required computer component. Most considered using a computer fun and they were pleased to have had the assignment. Some students said that sending e-mail responses to the instructor was faster than preparing a traditional assignment.
