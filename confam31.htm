Single parent, a hundred years ago, would have referred to a widow or widower with children. Today, the term can refer to any adult caring for a child, although we usually mean it as the adult who is solely legally responsible for most of a child’s care. Note that the parent need not be the only nurturer in the child’s life, however, and may not even live alone with the child. All of these scenarios could be labeled single parenting:

Parent living alone with child following divorce.
Parent living alone with child following death of other parent.
Never-married parent living alone with child 
Any of the above scenarios, but both living in another person’s home, such as a grandparent.
Parent and child living together, along with another adult, such as a boyfriend or girlfriend.
Parent not living with the child but contributing to the parenting of the child.

The point is made. Divorced dads who see their children once a month refer to themselves as single parents, just as divorced dads who provide the full-time home for their children. Single parent probably means nothing more than unmarried adult who has a child.

But there is a stereotype of the term—a single parent is a divorced woman who is rearing her child virtually unassisted. The stereotype does not reflect the experience of many families and perhaps few single parents’ self-descriptions.
