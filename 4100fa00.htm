Course Description:

Application of principles of family resource management.  Includes goal-setting, decision making, and time, energy, financial and consumer management.  (3 credit hours)

 

 

General Course Objectives:

The student will be able to:

1.           Discuss and apply family resource management theories and principles.

2.           Practice goal setting and decision making in relation to family resource management.

3.           Analyze and solve problems related to issues of family resource management.

4.           Apply principles of family time, energy, and financial management.

5.           Plan and analyze family consumer decisions.

Required Activities/Grading:

 

30-minute Presentations (10 percent): Working alone or with a partner, present material pertinent to family finances. Your presentation may be accomplished standing or seated—your choice. Overhead transparencies are an option, but so are manipulatives, graphics, and activities. Engage us. Guide us. Do not lecture us. Do not teach us. You are absolutely forbidden to spend any money on this presentation. OK. Not more than $5. Presenting alone, plan on 20-30 minutes. Presenting with a partner, plan on 40-60 minutes.

Undergraduates: draw from your own experience in financial matters or a topic from Goldsmith. Graduate students: use Kiyosaki as your text. Plan on 60 minutes (each) or create a combined presentation, workshop-style. Each graduate student should also submit a 5-page process paper on Kiyosaki.

 

30-second Presentations (5 percent): On class dates of your choosing, make five (5) presentations on any aspect of family resource management. Recommended time length: 30 seconds (roughly 50 words). Absolute time limit: 60 seconds (grade of F if you speak longer than one minute). You are absolutely forbidden to spend any money on these presentations! Typically, these will be presented at the start of each class period. If you anticipate working in community (or you already do), use these presentations to create polished projects you can use in the future with a specific audience. If you do not anticipate working in community, simply teach us something that interests you. (But keep the focus on family resource management!)

 

Track stocks weekly (10 percent): Each week, enter values for stocks you have elected to track over the course of the semester. Forms will be provided. Be prepared to speak about your portfolio each class period.

 

Purchasing Research (15 percent): Projects and due dates will be selected by students. Submit a plan by September 14 for approval by the instructor. In your plan, indicate whether you expect to present the project to the class as well as hand it in for grading. Suggested purchasing targets: a car (new or used), a computer system, a home.

 

Read texts. Come to class prepared to work as a collaborative study group. Essentially, this means having read the Goldsmith assignment for that day and having conceptualized in notes or drawings (one page per chapter) the main points as you see them. Conceptualizations should be captured on paper or overhead transparency. You will not hand these in for a grade; however, you will be expected to share them with the group during class time. EXCEPTION: for November 30, bring a conceptualization of only ONE chapter.

Additional Information

 

Assignments are due at the beginning of class periods. This time requirement applies to all assignments, including those submitted via e-mail. Papers should be typed. Five percent penalty will be assessed for each day (not class) an assignment is late. Work past one week late will not be accepted. My preference for virtually all work is submission via e-mail.

 

Attendance is expected. More than two unexcused absences will affect a semester grade. Be aware that test material is drawn heavily from class lectures and activities. Other aspects of evaluation (such as directions for papers and presentations) are covered in detail during class and only summarily in print.

 

Mid-term and final exams. Short-answer and objective questions will be drawn from class lectures, class activities, and the required texts. The tests are designed to allow students to demonstrate their knowledge, not to trip up students with trick questions.
