E-mail

Much of American business is conducted with electronic mail and, increasingly, so is higher education. Today’s educators offer “extended office hours” by way of e-mail, inviting students to make inquiries in a text-based mode. Since a professor’s answer can be sent back to the student with the push of a single button (“reply”), communication is faster and usually more clear than through use of telephone or voice mail.

Some professors ask that assignments be submitted electronically. Practical considerations include size of class, length of assignment, due dates within the semester, and computer lab access for students who do not own computers.

When electronic work is shared among all class members (distributing answers to a class e-mail list, for example), a unique form of group work emerges. Students and professors report enjoyment at taking part in stimulating discussion and sharing opinions in depth and in detail.

These benefits have been reported:

  • The medium promotes critical thinking and reflective communication.
  • Students experience more discussion than in-class time allows.
  • The relative anonymity of e-mail is attractive to some students who tend not to volunteer comments in class.
  • Asynchronous communication fits all students’ schedules.
  • Professors can observe the flow of communication before deciding how much or when to intervene with their own comments.
  • Skillbuilding in the use of computers.

Conferences

Synchronous Internet communication is variously called conferencing, chatting, and on-line discussing. Class meetings can be accomplished in this mode, or simply non-credit study group sessions. Students can receive immediate help with problems, especially if a professor or teaching assistant “sits in” on a session.

Participants must adjust to the distraction of scrolling text on their computer monitors and, usually, interruptions of other participants’ questions amid an on-going discussion. Most students consider these minor inconveniences.

Of greater concern is the risk of technical problems. Disruption on a university server can cancel a conference, causing the need to reschedule for all partcipants. Too, a computer problem for any one of the participants can be seen as a failure of the method.

Videoconference

A staple of distance learning, the videoconference allows students in remote locales to make eye contact with a professor and fellow students on other campuses. Sophisticated equipment utilizing either satellite transmission or telephone service typically provides views on 25" or larger color monitors and high quality sound. The teacher’s location may include computer, Internet access, and document camera to further enhance presentations.

While some students report discomfort at appearing “on TV,” professors and remote students generally agree that videoconference is the preferred medium for distance education. When combined with other uses of technology—specifically, communication via e-mail and delivery of course material through sites on the World Wide Web—videoconferencing can offer a full educational experience comparable in quality to on-campus courses.

High quality transmission of a videoconference is expensive. Studio equipment costs of tens of thousands of dollars; maintaining satellite transmission and even telephone line transmission requires salaries for support personnel. Despite the cost, universities make use of videoconferencing to reach students who would otherwise be unable to take courses. Increasing numbers of distant education students justify the expense of the technology.

Desktop videoconference, linking individual students from their own homes or local campuses, may provide the next generation of distance learning. Currently, software and a camera can be added to a personal computer for less than $500. Transmission is via the Internet or telephone lines. Video quality and audio quality are lacking, by popular standards. For example, most video options are limited to 10 - 20 frames per second, compared to the common usage of 30 frames per second for videotape.

Electronic Publications

Perhaps one of the most powerful uses of the Internet is the publishing of books and reports by both faculty and students. It is also an area of great debate as academia decides how to value such publishing and how to manage practical aspects of it.

Compared to print publishing, electronic or digital publishing offers greater speed to publication, lower cost, and wider distribution. All of these factors will encourage a larger number of writers. Whether readership will be increased is not known. However, writing and readership in narrow disciplines will almost certainly increase. When only a small audience (of a few hundred or a few thousand) is identified for a manuscript, print publishers are likely to pass on a project since recouping printing costs is not assured. By contrast, electronic publishers can produce the small-audience-document since the cost of posting it on the Internet is relatively small. Additionally, certain narrow audiences can be reached through the Internet, especially if computer users access material through filters which the publisher can identify as appropriate to the publication.

A major constraint in electronic publishing is reconciling current U.S. copyright law with common practices by Internet users. Those practices include duplication of copyrighted material, distribution of material without approval, and the selective use of elements from one work in the creation of a new work. For example, a graphic is easily “grabbed” from a web page, copied, and inserted in a new document. The sheer number of web pages in existence defies attempts at policing copyright infringement. Only the most widespread and most blatant unauthorized uses of material are likely to even be seen.

For academics, inexpensive electronic publishing is both boon and threat. On the one hand, many professors can publish their reputable works which have not been accepted by journals for a number of understandable reasons. On the other hand, many professors can publish their substandard works which have been rejected by journals for good reason. In short, any one can post any document, regardless of its quality or worth.

Peer review journals, generally acknowledged as the safest conduits for publication of research, may translate to the Internet in the future. Indeed, in some fields of study, electronic journals are already the norm. For most academics, however, the print journal remains the appropriate publisher as the professor seeks recognition in a field or tenure from a university. How Internet journals will rank among publications has not been addressed yet. Presumably, universities will assign some value to electronic publication as more faculty members present their papers and books in that format and ask that they be recognized as scholarly work.

Electronic publishing allows

  • the publishing of many new books from many more authors,
  • quick revision of material,
  • distribution outside of the traditional book market,
  • publishing in narrow disciplines to small audiences,
  • textbooks customizable to individual courses,
  • increased number of color graphics,
  • animation and sound to accompany text,
  • display formats which can be customized by each reader.
