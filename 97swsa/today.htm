Today’s undergraduates in the field of aging are varied in age and experience. The great equalizer for these students is the challenge of utilizing technology for the benefit of their studies and, eventually, of their clients.

Virtually all students today have access to computers in university labs.

Increasing numbers of students use home computers, and those who do generally prefer their own over university computers.

Traditional (younger) college students come to campus as experienced computer users. National Center for Education Statistics from 1993 show that more than a quarter of American schoolchildren use computers at home, and more than half use computers at school. These figures suggest that Americans’ exposure to computers is sufficient to allow the incorporation of computer technology in the undergraduate curriculum.

Nontraditional (older) students have proved as competent as their younger counterparts in computer skill building and creative uses of educational technology. In our own survey of students at TWU, we found that older students maintain positive attitude toward the use of computers in coursework and expect to use computers in their education and in their careers.
