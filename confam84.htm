American Women

  • Earnings and Jobs

    1998 Real median earnings of women and men who worked full time, year-round: $25,862 for women and $35,345 for men.

    In 1998, women earned 73 cents for every dollar earned by men, not statistically different from the high of 74 cents in 1996. 

    Among 25-to-34-year olds, women earned 82 cents for every dollar earned by men.

    The percentage of women in the paid labor force increased from 32% in 1947 to 48% in 1997.

    Between 1951 and 1997, the proportion of wives who were in the labor force nearly tripled, from 23% to 62%.

  • Population Distribution

    Females outnumber males nationally, except in four states: Alaska, Hawaii, Nevada and Wyoming.

    In 1997, the life expectancy for women was 79 years; for men, it was 74 years. 

    Projections for 2010 show life expectancy will be 81 years for women and 74 years for men.

    Women are increasingly living alone.


     

  • Motherhood

    In 1995, 58% of 15- to 44-year-old women had given birth to at least one child: 18% to one child, 23% to two, 11% to three and 6% to four or more.

    55% of women with a newborn were in the labor force in 1995, up from 31% in 1976. The figure was 68% if they had at least a bachelor’s degree.

    More women nowadays are either postponing or not ever having children.

  • Marriage and Family
    In 1998, 58% of women 18 years and older were married, 21% had never married, 11% each were widowed and 11% were divorced.

    The estimated median age at first marriage was 25.0 years for women in 1998.

    Single mothers in 1998 = 9.8 million.

    Single mothers in 1970 = 3.4 million.

    Mothers account for most of the nation’s 11.9 million single parents.

    The number of women living alone doubled between 1970 and 1998, to 14% of all women.

    About 30% of households in 1998 were maintained by women with no husband present. In 1970, the figure was about 20%.

    In 1997, about 2.9 million grandmothers lived with their grandchildren compared with 1.7 million grandfathers. Of these grandmothers, 340,000 were raising their grandchildren without a grandfather or the children’s parents present.

 

  • Sports and Recreation

    During the 1996-97 school year, women accounted for 40% of participation in NCAA-sanctioned sports.  NCAA women’s teams virtually equal the number of men’s teams. Outdoor track has the most female athletes; basketball has the most women’s teams.

    More than 2.5 million girls took part in high school athletic programs during the 1997-98 school year, triple the number in 1972-73. Participation levels by boys remained about the same during the same span of years.

 

  • Computer Use
    In 1997, women used computers on the job more often than men, 57% to 44%.

    In 1997, 20% of women reported they used the Internet; 25% of men reported using it.

    There was no difference between girls’ and boys’ computer use in 1997. 83% of girls with a computer at home used it and 70% used one at school.
