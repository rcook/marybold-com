Blended ethnicity within a family is a way to refer to members of various skin pigment, various ethnic background, various national heritage, and so forth. In parts of American society, “racial identifiers” are requested on forms of all kinds: applications, school records, surveys. Even when labeled as optional, such identifiers may carry powerful messages. For many, the choices of “races” printed on forms are not relevant or do not match their self-identity. Some children, for example, struggle with a form’s implied requirement to choose between mother’s identifier and father’s. 

Even the use of the word “race” is problematic. The term has little meaning in the biological sciences. The variation of individuals within a specified “race” is greater than the difference between “race A” and “race B.” In everyday society the word is often used to refer to ethnicity or national heritage. Replacing it with “ethnicity” makes sense. 

In the family field, many researchers continue to identify “race” in research questions and in sampling. Frequently, the effect is that “race” is a proxy for SES. Research is strengthened when proxy words are not used. Ethnicity may be important to research, of course. When it is, it should be identified with considered choice of words that may or may not include the word “race.”
