Linked households is a tactful replacement for “broken homes,” a term that can generate a broken nose as easily as a nod of the head.

(“Broken homes” does not offend everyone, obviously. However, it has become an automatic phrase by many who have never actually considered the words. A good rule of thumb is to reflect on the meanings a child might place on the phrase. If a child would be confused or, worse, worried about the phrase, then it should probably be replaced. That doesn’t mean we have to sugar-coat our words. After all, “divorce” is a logical noun and verb that shouldn’t be replaced with your parents’ decision to terminate their marital commitment. But “broken home” connotes a lot more than “divorce.”)

In 1971, Paul Bohannon wrote of “divorce chains” as the pathways between households that maintained ties after divorce. More than twenty years later, Andrew Cherlin and Frank Furstenberg revisited the vocabulary question and suggested that “remarriage chains” is more accurate. The concept of chain or link is especially helpful today as we chart the connections between many households and across many generations. Four and even five generations are becoming the norm for families, increasing the number and scope of family links.

Another factor promoting linked households is the gradual decline in fertility rates. As young families opt to have fewer children, the older generations may want to increase their contact (links) with any child they can. For example, my grown children may decide to not have children. But they might marry people who have children. As an aging woman in search of grandchildren, I might pursue a relationship with those step-grandchildren, adding another link to their already-existing chain that has two sets of grandparents. In this way, my home would become linked to theirs, in spite of the fact that I had not divorced or remarried, and, in fact, my son or daughter hadn’t either.
