Course Description:

Application of principles of family resource management.  Includes goal-setting, decision making, and time, energy, financial and consumer management.  (3 credit hours)

 

 

General Course Objectives:

The student will be able to:

1.           Discuss and apply family resource management theories and principles.

2.           Practice goal setting and decision making in relation to family resource management.

3.           Analyze and solve problems related to issues of family resource management.

4.           Apply principles of family time, energy, and financial management.

5.           Plan and analyze family consumer decisions.

 

 

Required Course Readings:

 

Goldsmith, E. (1996). Resource management for individuals and families. New York: West. (Fireside). We will consume virtually all of this book. Emphasis will be on vocabulary, which will be the basis for exams.

 

Zelenak, M.J. (1999). Consumer Economies: The Consumer in our Society. Scottsdale, AR: Holcomb & Hathaway. We will draw selectively from this book. Students will be responsible for presenting chapters 1, 2, 9, 12, 14, 15, 16, Glossary.

Required Activities/Grading:

 

Presentation from Zelenak (10 percent): Working alone or with a partner, present material from Zelenak. (Choices will be made at the first class meeting.) Your presentation may be accomplished standing or seated—your choice. Overhead transparencies are an option, but so are manipulatives, graphics, and activities. Engage us. Guide us. Do not lecture us. Do not teach us. Although you may borrow ideas from the instructor’s style of presenting material, you are absolutely forbidden to spend any money on this presentation. OK. Not more than $5.

 

30-second presentations (5 percent): On class dates of your choosing, make five (5) presentations on any aspect of family resource management. Recommended time length: 30 seconds (roughly 50 words). Absolute time limit: 60 seconds (grade of F if you speak longer than one minute). You are absolutely forbidden to spend any money on these presentations! Typically, these will be presented at the start of each class period. If you anticipate working in community (or you already do), use these presentations to create polished projects you can use in the future with a specific audience. If you do not anticipate working in community, simply teach us something that interests you. (But keep the focus on family resource management!)

 

Track stocks weekly (10 percent): Each week, enter values for stocks you have elected to track over the course of the semester. On September 9, the instructor will provide newspaper stock listings to assist. Thereafter, no provisions will be made by the instructor.

 

Holiday observation/project (15 percent): Projects and due dates will be selected by students. Submit a typed plan by September 30 for approval by the instructor. In your plan, indicate whether you expect to present the project to the class as well as hand it in for grading. Students who anticipate going to graduate school in the future are strongly urged to write a research paper for this project in order to gain practice in scholarly writing.

 

Read texts. Come to class prepared to work as a collaborative study group. Essentially, this means having read the Goldsmith assignment for that day and having conceptualized in notes or drawings (one page per chapter) the main points as you see them. Conceptualizations should be captured on paper or overhead transparency. You will not hand these in for a grade; however, you will be expected to share them with the group during class time. EXCEPTION: for December 2, bring a conceptualization of only ONE chapter.

Additional Information

 

Assignments are due at the beginning of class periods. This time requirement applies to all assignments, including those submitted via e-mail. Papers should be typed. Five percent penalty will be assessed for each day (not class) an assignment is late. Work past one week late will not be accepted.

 

Attendance is expected. For each absence after one, five points will be deducted from the semester point total. Two points will be deducted for each tardy past two. Be aware that test material is drawn heavily from class lectures and activities. Other aspects of evaluation (such as directions for papers and presentations) are covered in detail during class and only summarily in print.

 

Mid-term and final exams. Short-answer and objective questions will be drawn from class lectures, class activities, and the required texts. The tests are designed to allow students to demonstrate their knowledge, not to trip up students with trick questions.
