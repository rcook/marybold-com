Beanpole family composition

Historically, American society could be depicted as a population pyramid. The broad base represented the large number of children born. The mid section reflected a typical death rate for generations born before modern medicine. The top of the pyramid represented the small number of old people still alive.

Today, the pyramid is more like a big block. There is little tapering off even near the top (the aged) of the population. For families, this means that several generations are alive at the same time.

A beanpole depicts this situation: at the top is the oldest generation (in this drawing we might label that G1 for Generation #1) and at the bottom is the youngest (G5). The bean plant has growth at specific points — birth cohorts about 20 - 25 years apart.

In the block of the current American population, the beanpole is becoming typical across the entire society. In the historical pyramid shape, a beanpole would occur for a narrow segment of the population. The longest-lived family line would run from the base to the apex, but the pyramid could not accommodate very many tall beanpoles running up the center of it.
