Good dad/bad dad was coined by Frank Furstenberg to describe the contradictory view of fathers in contemporary America. 

“Good” dads participate in their children’s upbringing. (See also fathering.)

“Bad” dads, by contrast, leave their families and do not provide any type of support for their children.

Such a contrast of fathers has become a stark descriptor of American society. For some fathers, the good dad/bad dad syndrome is even more stark—because they may be both types simultaneously. 

The acronym SOD has developed to refer to “start-over dads” who, in a second marriage, are rearing children differently than in the first marriage. The term invariably indicates that with the second family, the man is a “good” father, spending more time with children. This is sometimes attributed to the father being in a more established stage of career and therefore enjoying more leisure.
