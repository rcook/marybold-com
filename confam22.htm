GLBT family is a term slowly gaining in popularity to refer to families headed by parents who describe their sexual orientation as gay, lesbian, bisexual, or transgender (GLBT). A variation—LGBT—also appears in both scholarly and lay literature.

Judith Stacey (1998) called this family form the only new structure to emerge in many centuries. In the U.S., attention to GLBT families arose in the 1980s. Of course, throughout human history homosexuals have parented children. What is new is the open acknowledgment of GLBT parents, frequently residing with same-sex partners.

Research in the 1990s essentially supported the notion that children in GLBT homes develop normally and happily. One way of looking at this is the typical description of family life in America: people with kids spend their time at school carnivals, soccer practice, and Disney movies. These events are the constants that dominate family life—not parents’ sexual orientation. Thus, GLBT families are busy with much the same activity as all other families.

GLBT families do face special problems, however, starting with what terminology to use to describe parents in the home. For one lesbian couple, the terms of choice are “Mom” and “Leesa,” who is described outside the home as “my step-mom.” For another couple, the terms of choice are “Mom” and “Uncle Paula.”  (See also co-parent.)

Among other problems that some GLBT families face are:

  • social stigma, especially if parents are closeted (Stacey, 1998)

  • fear of problems at school (Hare, 1994)

  • perceived need for secrecy (Sigenthaler, 1999)

  • same-sex parents are rarely defined as a family (Hare, 1994)

  • classroom teachers may not be supportive (Koerner & Hulsebosch, 1996)

  • some teachers do not pick up on language clues (Wickens, 1993)
